# 9607 Theory Provenance

**Which thinkers are Cambridge's, which are ours, and what the difference means.**

Compiled while building the concept layer, because the question kept recurring and the answer is not obvious from any single document.

## The headline

> **Neither syllabus names a theorist. Not one personal name appears in the 2027 syllabus or the 2028–30 syllabus.**

Every theorist associated with 9607 comes from either a Scheme of Work's *suggested teaching activities* column, or from the centre.

This is not an oversight. Cambridge states the policy once, explicitly, in the Postmodern media topic (Syllabus 2027 p.18; retained verbatim in the 2028–30 syllabus):

> "**We do not prioritise one theory or approach over another, and candidates are encouraged to explore a range of approaches and perspectives, across a range of media forms.**"

⚠ **The suggested roster changed with the 2028 Scheme of Work** (ingested 2026-07-20). The from-2021 SoW named eight thinkers; the 2028 SoW names ten — adding **Van Zoonen, hooks, Gilroy and Alvarado** (as representation theorists, surnames only), recasting **Hall** from an encoding/decoding Extension activity into that same representation list, and **dropping McLuhan and Postman** along with the Media ecology genealogy. Details in Tier 1 below.

## But theory is still required

The distinction that matters, and the one that gets lost:

| | Required? | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Using **theory** | **Yes** | "use key theories of media studies and specialist subject-specific terminology appropriately" (Syllabus p.13); AO2 requires "applying knowledge and understanding of **theoretical** and creative approaches" (p.12) |
| Using **specific named theorists** | **No** | No name appears in the syllabus; Cambridge "does not prioritise one theory or approach over another" (p.18) |

**"No prescribed theorists" ≠ "theory optional."** A candidate must apply theoretical approaches; which ones is the centre's choice. The corollary for marking: credit attaches to *application*, never to the name-check. A precisely applied theory the examiner has never heard of beats a name-dropped canonical one.

The syllabus also devolves the choice explicitly: "you are responsible for selecting resources and examples, texts and subject contexts to support your learners' study" (p.13).

Only **Jameson** gets a usable framework. The four new names arrive in a single clause about theories of representation applied to "class, age, gender, ethnicity" — identification of *which* Hall/hooks/Gilroy/Alvarado ideas to use is left entirely to the centre.

What the 2028 SoW changed:

- **Hall recast.** The encoding/decoding Extension activity is gone; Hall now appears only in the representation-theorists list. Encoding/decoding is no longer suggested anywhere in the 2028 SoW.
- **McLuhan and Postman dropped.** The "traced back to" genealogy sentence disappears with the renamed C4 Section B (Media ecology → Changing media environments). No thinker at all is named in the new synoptic topic.
- **Baudrillard half-arrives.** The Postmodern media resource list now links "Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy – Jean Baudrillard" — a resource pointer, not an attribution. No idea of Baudrillard's is stated.
- Mulvey, Propp and Todorov remain unnamed in every Cambridge document — still BNDS additions (Tier 2).

## Tier 2 — BNDS additions

Taught in the Semester 1 scheme, absent from every Cambridge document held here:

| Thinker | Where taught | Purpose |
|---|---|---|

| Vladimir Propp | L05, L06 | character functions; "maps characters by function rather than identity" |
| Laura Mulvey | [[classes/media-studies/lesson-plans/9607-s1-lesson-09-the-gaze\|L09]] | the male gaze, "tested across decades" |

**Van Zoonen and bell hooks were moved out of this tier on 2026-07-20**: the 2028 Scheme of Work names both (surname only) as representation theorists, so the L09 gaze block is now half Cambridge-suggested (van Zoonen, hooks) and half centre-chosen (Mulvey). The *specific ideas* taught in L09 — gender as performance, the three looks, the oppositional gaze — are still attributed by no Cambridge document.

All of these are legitimate under the pluralism policy. All should be **labeled as choices** when taught — partly for intellectual honesty, and partly because it changes how students write: a chosen framework has to be argued for and stress-tested, not cited as authority.

The BNDS design already builds this in. L06 hunts for "where the theory strains, because strain is what a top-band answer discusses"; L09 frames gaze theory "as live questions, not settled answers" and uses hooks to ask "who the text never expected to be looking." That is exactly the right posture for a syllabus with no prescribed canon.

## What this means for planning

**1. The theory shelf is ours to stock.** Cambridge supplies eight names, one framework, and no readings. Everything else is a centre decision, and the vault currently holds **no primary or secondary reading for any theorist** — `raw/media-studies/resources/readings/` is empty.

**2. The synoptic topic now has method but still no theory.** Under the from-2021 SoW, media ecology named its two founders (McLuhan, Postman) while attributing no idea to either, and cited no external links. The 2028 SoW's **Changing media environments** goes the other way: it drops the founders entirely, supplies a structured method (three-era periodization, four focus areas, synoptic-links activities) and adds resource links (Nielsen Norman Group, Pew, Ofcom) — but names **zero thinkers** for a compulsory 30-mark topic. The theory layer remains wholly the centre's to stock; what changed is that Cambridge no longer even gestures at whose theory it might be. See [[concepts/media-ecology]]. Still Grade 12, Semester 4 content by date.

**3. Teach application, not attribution.** Since nothing is prescribed, no mark is available for knowing a name. The mark is in the anchoring — Cambridge's own phrasing: "anchoring the arguments in specific texts or phenomena is key to demonstrating a sophisticated understanding" (SoW p.24), and candidates must "relate concrete examples to these theoretical perspectives" (SoW p.22).

**4. For ESL learners this is good news.** A short, well-drilled set of theories applied precisely will outscore a long list half-remembered. The syllabus permits exactly that.

## Method note

Established by reading the 2027 syllabus (43pp), the from-2021 Scheme of Work (36pp) and the Coursework Handbook (28pp) in full, searching for personal names and checking each occurrence's column placement. Extended 2026-07-20 by the same name-search over the 2028 Scheme of Work (.docx) and the 2028–30 syllabus. The prescribed/suggested distinction is structural: the Scheme of Work's three-column tables put syllabus references and assessment objectives in columns 1–2, and everything else — including all eight theorists — in column 3, headed "Suggested teaching activities."

Four bulleted lists sit inside column 3 but are flagged as syllabus requirements by their lead-ins ("as required by the syllabus", "The focus for examination questions will be on", "Key areas in the examination are", "questions focussing on"). **Not one of those four prescribed lists names a theorist.** That contrast is the strongest internal evidence that theory is scaffolding rather than content — though it is an inference from document structure, not a stated rule.
