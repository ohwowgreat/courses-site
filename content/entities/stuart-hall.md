# Stuart Hall

Named in both Schemes of Work — but **for different things**. The from-2021 SoW names him for **encoding and decoding**; the 2028 SoW recasts him as a **representation theorist** and drops encoding/decoding entirely.

## What Cambridge attributes to him

**from-2021 SoW (the G11/AS support):**

> "**Extension activity:** explore **Stuart Hall's** ideas of encoding and decoding when making sense of a media text" (SoW p.14)

The Scheme of Work supplies a video link and no further explanation — it does not name the three reading positions, and neither does any syllabus.

**2028 SoW (ingested 2026-07-20):**

> "candidates need to learn the concept of Representation - best done with reference to theories of representation where there is a variety of student-friendly materials available - such as **Hall, Van Zoonen, hooks, Gilroy, Alvarado**." (C2 Section A camera row)

⚠ **The encoding/decoding extension does not appear anywhere in the 2028 SoW.** Hall survives the revision, but what Cambridge suggests him *for* has shifted from reception to representation — presumably his work on stereotyping and the racialized regime of representation, though the 2028 SoW attributes no specific idea. Both suggestions remain suggestions; neither syllabus names him.

## Why he matters here

The syllabus requires that candidates understand "how audiences interpret the media, including how they may interpret the same texts in different ways, using a variety of theories or ideologies" (Syllabus 2027 p.15) — without naming a theory. Hall's model is Cambridge's own suggested answer to that bullet.

The standard three positions (dominant, negotiated, oppositional) are widely taught but **are not stated in any Cambridge document held in this vault.** Treat them as course-standard knowledge rather than as a Cambridge citation.

## Its status in the course

Filed as an **Extension activity**, which in the Scheme of Work's own key means differentiated content for stronger learners (SoW p.4): "Opportunities for differentiation are indicated as Extension activities."

So Cambridge does not treat encoding/decoding as core AS content. It is, however, the cleanest bridge from audience-as-target to audience-as-interpreter, and it feeds forward into [[concepts/power-and-the-media]], where the question of whose reading counts becomes the topic itself.

## The teaching move

Take one text the class genuinely disagrees about, then map their actual readings onto the three positions. The theory stops being abstract the moment their own argument is the evidence — and it converts a classroom disagreement into exam-usable analysis.
