# Media Regulation

One of the three **A Level Media debates** topics; candidates must study at least two of the three (Syllabus 2027 pp.9, 18). Examined in Component 4 Section A as one 15-mark essay, "referring to one or more media forms" (p.26).

Syllabus framing: "Media regulation considers issues of morality and decency, accuracy of content and media ownership" (p.18).

The core question: **who should control what media can say and show, and does control actually work?**

## What candidates must study (Syllabus 2027 p.18)

- the nature of contemporary and historical media regulation
- the arguments for and against specific forms of media regulation
- the effectiveness of regulatory practices, including the ability to impose or recommend sanctions
- the wider social issues relating to media regulation
- the roles of different regulatory bodies in relation to ownership, morality and accuracy
- the legal and ethical frameworks within which media producers and distributors must operate

The Scheme of Work reproduces the last five as "Other areas of focus **as required by the syllabus**" (SoW p.22) — its own flag that these are prescribed rather than suggested.

Note the second bullet: **"arguments for and against."** This topic is assessed as a two-sided argument, not a description of a regulatory system. A candidate who explains how regulation works but never argues about whether it should is answering the wrong question.

## Sub-topics (SoW p.23, suggested)

"Regulation could include: taste and decency · illegality and incitement to crime · ownership and pluralism · intellectual property and piracy · public service and responsibilities to the nation."

The internet complicates all of them: "The debates about internet regulation are particularly pertinent and action is problematic because of its lack of central organisation and the fact that it is an international medium, so does not fall under a particular jurisdiction" (SoW p.23).

## What the 2028 Scheme of Work adds (ingested 2026-07-20)

The core text — the opening discussion questions, the sensitivity advice, the decade-timeline activity, the sub-topics list — carries over essentially verbatim, with one addition to the alternative-approach sentence: regulation can now also focus on "**misinformation**" alongside intellectual property, ownership and piracy. On top of the carryover, the 2028 edition appends a structured activity bank:

- **Regulatory authority comparison** — two regulatory organisations compared for legal authority, enforcement powers, sanctions, criticisms, "challenges in the digital era" (none is named — the choice is the centre's)
- **Regulation in context** — trigger / technological context / reactive-or-proactive / intended and unintended outcomes for key regulatory developments
- **Regulatory sanctions case study** — breach, speed of response, severity, impact, public trust
- **Ideological debate** — assigned positions (free market, public service, critical theory) arguing "The media should be strictly regulated to protect society"
- **Regulatory gap mapping** — streaming, social media, influencer marketing, UGC; jurisdiction issues and "platform vs publisher responsibility"
- **Global regulation comparison** — "Is national regulation still effective in a globalised media industry?"
- **Question planning workshop** — command words, argument strands, counter-argument, peer-assessed against AO1/AO2

The digital-era framing (platform vs publisher, algorithmic content, converged environments) is new; the from-2021 edition predated all of it.

## ⚠ Cambridge's own sensitivity advice

This is the only topic in the whole Scheme of Work that carries a handling caveat, and it does so twice — **retained unchanged in the 2028 edition**, including the UK-model fallback.

> "It is preferable to study regulation in your country/region. However, **this may not always be possible because of the sensitivity of the subject.** If this is the case, and you still wish to explore this topic, it is suggested that you encourage learners to research **the UK model**. There is plenty of material available on the Internet to facilitate this approach. **Alternatively, an approach to regulation could be taken which focuses on intellectual property, ownership, piracy or misinformation – issues of morality are only one dimension.**" (SoW p.22)

And on the historical timeline activity: "(Once again, this needs to be handled sensitively according to the political situation of the country/region where the material is being delivered.)" (SoW p.22)

**Three routes are therefore sanctioned by Cambridge itself:** study your own region (preferred); substitute the UK model; or reframe onto IP, ownership, piracy and misinformation rather than morality and press freedom.

**BNDS position:** the third route, or the UK model studied comparatively, is the workable path. This is not an evasion of the syllabus — it is the alternative Cambridge names in its own document. Recorded in the course map's decision tracker; the working pair at BNDS is currently [[concepts/postmodern-media]] + [[concepts/power-and-the-media]], with regulation as the documented swap option.

## Suggested teaching activities (SoW pp.22–23)

- **Opening discussion:** "How is Media regulated where you live? / Has Media always been regulated in this way? / What effect does Media regulation have on consumption? / Are there different ways of regulating the Media? / How do other countries/regions regulate their Media?"
- **Historical timeline:** groups take different decades of the 20th/21st centuries and different media (TV, Film, Print, Radio, Online), research developments in regulation during their period, and present; findings combine into "a visual timeline of the historical context of regulation."
- **Comparative research:** regulation in other countries, "especially the UK and the USA," compared with their own.
- **Independent case studies**, then shared "in preparation for responding to an unseen question about media regulation."

## ⚠ Cambridge names nobody — still true in 2028

The Media regulation section remains the emptiest in the document for provenance: **no theorist, no theory, and not a single regulatory body named in the topic itself.** No BBFC, no IPSO, no NRTA — the 2028 activities say "learners select two regulatory organisations" and leave the selection open. The only movement: **Ofcom now appears as a resource link elsewhere in the 2028 SoW** ("Ofcom – Media Ownership Rules" under the Power topic; "Ofcom – Online Nation Report" under Changing media environments).

Any regulator, framework or thinker in a lesson plan for this topic is a centre addition. See [[analyses/9607-theory-provenance]].
