# Production Craft

The AO3 curriculum. **AO3 is 40% of the whole A Level and sits entirely inside the two portfolios** (Syllabus 2027 p.12) — production weeks are the highest-yield weeks of the course.

The method in both Cambridge documents is unambiguous: skills are built through **short, discrete, self-contained exercises before the assessed production begins**, not through one large project. A phone camera is enough for all of them (SoW p.9).

*2028 Scheme of Work check (2026-07-20): the entire C1 and C3 exercise bank below carries over essentially verbatim — same camera, editing, sound, print and pitch exercises for both briefs and all four C3 options. The 2028 edition adds a C1 resource list (StudioBinder shot types, Canva storyboard templates, Issuu, Every Frame a Painting, film-terms glossaries) and refreshed links. ⚠ Its process wording (blog, critical reflection, social media page) is stale against the 2028–30 syllabus — the exercises transfer, the evidence format does not; see [[concepts/process-evidence-and-reflection]].*

## Camera exercises (SoW p.9)

Framing: "a series of short, self-contained exercises with camera (or even phone) can combine technical elements with story elements."

- "a slow tilt up to reveal character and create suspense"
- "an establishing shot to set the scene"
- "a close-up (CU) to indicate significance (or extreme close-up if particularly significant)"
- "a canted angle / Dutch tilt to indicate dissonance"
- "simulation of point of view (POV)"

Plus the shoot-from-a-storyboard drill: "some frames of an existing, or pre-prepared, storyboard can be provided and learners asked to shoot from it. The results can be presented and critiqued in class."

Note the design principle — each exercise pairs a **technique** with a **meaning**. None of them is "practise a close-up."

## Editing exercises (SoW pp.9–10)

Scale rule: "These exercises can be developed into editing short sequences, just two or three shots to begin with."

- "someone looking off-screen followed by the object they are looking at"
- "shot / reverse shot for a conversation between two people (which is also an opportunity to highlight the 180-degree rule)"
- "a cut from an exterior establishing shot to an interior, suggesting spatial continuity"
- "cross-cutting between two different actions in order to suggest a connection"

## Sound exercises (SoW p.10)

- **Foley** — "incidental sound effects which are created artificially to enhance the drama, but which are diegetic… e.g. footsteps, gunshots, punches and kisses"
- Post-production voice: "Voices (breathing, gasping, for example) may also be added in post-production"
- Non-diegetic demonstration: students present short edited sequences and the class discusses the effect of different music choices
- **Re-cut trailers** — "the impact on perception of genre is clear." The fastest way to prove that editing and music construct genre

## Print and campaign craft (SoW pp.11–12)

- Photograph for a new music magazine cover, then present it with a rationale: "they should experiment with various angles, poses and post-production treatments"
- Devise a title for a new magazine and discuss "the connotations of the title"
- Given a title, devise a strapline, with rationale
- Devise coverlines, then draft the opening paragraphs of those articles

The Coursework Handbook adds the layout drills (CWH pp.11–12): the terminology matching game, box-mapping where text falls on a page, and a **mood board** for "house style, use of colours, formatting of page numbers, fonts and images."

**House style** is the print equivalent of continuity: it is named as a mark-bearing requirement — "in the case of the magazine option, present a clear house style" (CWH p.7) — and it appears on the peer-feedback form.

## Story before shooting

Even for a two-minute product, the Scheme of Work insists on story groundwork (SoW p.10):

> "it's worth doing some basic work on story along with genre – learners should be able to explain what sort of film this is; who the main character is; what the central conflict is likely to be; the questions or enigmas that the opening creates – that is, what information is given? What is withheld? What clues are provided?"

The Coursework Handbook gives the build order for storyboarding (CWH p.12): "plan the narrative, then the mise en scene/generic conventions, then the camera shots, sound, movement and finally the transitions."

And a planning tool worth copying — a table with rows for *Elements / Symbolic codes / Technical codes / Genre / Mise-en-scène / Transition* against each sequence, introduced as "a useful way to encourage learners to avoid a narrative approach and engage with the media strategies required at this level" (CWH p.12). It is the clearest picture either document gives of what a good planning document looks like.

## The research-to-production rule

The load-bearing sentence (CWH p.12):

> "The production plan should draw on the research stage and should evidence decision-making based on media techniques, codes and conventions that have been identified."

Paired with: "Creative choices should be based on research, and evidenced and justified in the blog" (CWH p.7). And the condition on breaking rules: if students challenge conventions, "they **must** justify and document it in both their blog, and their creative critical reflection work" (CWH p.12).

**Every production decision should be traceable to something researched.** That is the rule to teach on day one, because it is what criterion 4 of the mark scheme rewards.

## Peer review

The Coursework Handbook supplies a six-prompt feedback form for screening drafts (CWH p.13), including "What might stop this product being viewed as a professional product for publication?" and "If you were to re-edit this product what would your top three priorities be?"

It also advises reserving time for the re-edit: "two or three hours should enable learners to re-film a sequence after feedback and complete a reasonable level of editing before submitting" (CWH p.13). Advisory, not a rule — but a reflow that eats the re-edit window costs marks in criterion 2 and 3.

## Two rules that constrain the room

- **Acting is not assessed.** "As acting is not a skill that is assessed in media studies… groups may use personnel external to the group to appear in their productions in photos, video or audio. **The ability of candidates to direct actors is part of the assessment**" (Syllabus 2027 p.30). Students may cast anyone; they are marked on direction, not performance.
- **Teachers must not design the product.** "while you should not offer suggestions for production, you might consider recommending relevant materials to facilitate this critical engagement" (CWH p.8). The line is: recommend sources, never solutions.

## Originality

For C1 (Syllabus 2027 p.21): all images and text original; sound "predominantly original"; music from an acknowledged source permitted. For C3 (p.24): "Images and video should be original material only. Sound should be predominantly original."

⚠ The Coursework Handbook's version — "the images and video for this production must be original… the use of music taken from an acknowledged source is permissible" (CWH p.7) — sits in a paragraph whose surrounding text reads Advanced-Portfolio-specific, so its scope is ambiguous *in that document*. The syllabus states it cleanly for both components; cite the syllabus.

Neither document specifies **how** music must be acknowledged — no format, placement or credit rule exists.

Generative AI: "Cambridge International has a policy on the use of generative AI by candidates in coursework. The inappropriate use of AI should be treated as a form of plagiarism" (Syllabus 2027 p.30). Under the 2028–30 spec AI images become permitted with documented prompts and justification — see [[classes/media-studies/media-studies]].
