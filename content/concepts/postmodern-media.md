# Postmodern Media

One of the three **A Level Media debates** topics (Syllabus 2027 pp.9, 18). One of the two BNDS has provisionally chosen, alongside [[concepts/power-and-the-media]].

The core question: **is the distinction between reality and representation collapsing, and does it matter?**

## The syllabus framing (p.18)

> "Postmodernism can be characterised by modes such as pastiche, parody, intertextuality, self-referentiality and a conscious engagement with other texts. **We do not prioritise one theory or approach over another, and candidates are encouraged to explore a range of approaches and perspectives, across a range of media forms.**"

That second sentence is the clearest statement anywhere in the syllabus about the role of theory in 9607 — see [[analyses/9607-theory-provenance]].

## What candidates must study (Syllabus 2027 p.18)

- the different versions of postmodernism (historical period, style, theoretical approach)
- the arguments for and against understanding some forms of media as postmodern
- the ways postmodern media texts can challenge traditional relationships between texts and audiences
- the relationship between postmodernism and popular culture
- the ways media audiences and industries operate differently in a postmodern world
- the relationship between postmodernism and narrative

The Scheme of Work reproduces the last five as "The focus for examination questions will be on" (SoW p.24).

**"Arguments for and against understanding some forms of media as postmodern"** is doing real work here. The examinable position includes *skepticism* — a candidate may argue that postmodernism is an unhelpful lens, provided they argue it with evidence.

## Cambridge concedes the concept is unstable

> "Postmodernism is notoriously tricky to pin down… it can refer to **a general condition of contemporary life** in which representations, reality, surfaces and styles are chaotically intermingled, or it can refer to **specific modes of communication** which are arch, self-referential and knowing (irony is often referred to as exemplary of the postmodern mode)." (SoW p.23)

That two-way split — condition versus textual mode — is the Scheme of Work's own framing and a good essay structure in itself.

## The two named thinkers

**[[entities/guy-debord]]** (SoW p.23) — *The Society of the Spectacle* (1967), "a critique of media-saturated capitalist culture… still relevant in diagnosing aspects of the postmodern condition."

**[[entities/fredric-jameson]]** (SoW pp.23–24) — "diagnoses the postmodern condition as characterised by":

- "An ahistorical mode (lacking a sense of the past – history becomes another set of signifiers which can be played with)."
- "A lack of distinction between 'high' and 'low' culture."
- "Surfaces – increasingly there is no time for depth – politics becomes soundbites, protests become slogans, the world is a collection of images."
- "Affectlessness – the predominant mode of irony and 'knowingness' leaves little room for real emotion, but short, sharp intense emotional responses are elicited through surface representations of things of which people have no first-hand experience, e.g.: Brexit, the death of Diana, refugees."
- "Technologies of reproduction and recirculation, e.g.: all social media."

Those five are the most usable analytical checklist in the whole of Component 4 — each one converts directly into an essay paragraph with a contemporary example.

## What the 2028 Scheme of Work adds (ingested 2026-07-20)

The conceptual spine carries over — the "notoriously tricky" instability framing, Debord and Jameson verbatim, the anchoring-in-texts technique note. The old activity set (foraging, the TV-news spectacle exercise, the pastiche/parody extension) is replaced by a structured bank:

- **Conceptual framework comparison** — postmodernism as historical period vs stylistic mode vs theoretical approach, tabulated with media examples (this operationalizes the syllabus's "different versions" bullet directly)
- **Textual feature analysis** — pastiche, parody, intertextuality, self-referentiality, genre hybridity identified in one chosen text
- **Structured critical debate** — "Contemporary media is fundamentally postmodern" vs "Postmodernism is an overused or outdated concept" (the skepticism position now has its own exercise)
- **Narrative deconstruction** — linear vs postmodern structure: non-linearity, fragmentation, ambiguity, open endings
- **Text/audience relationships** — participatory culture, fan remixing, meme circulation; "whether this increases or commodifies audience power"
- **Intertextuality mapping** — network diagram of references, borrowings, parodies; whether intertextuality "supports commercial strategies"
- **Essay planning workshop** — command word, analytical strands, counterarguments

New resource links include the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy and — for the first time in any Cambridge 9607 document — a **Jean Baudrillard** pointer (Stanford Encyclopedia entry). A link, not an attribution; see [[analyses/9607-theory-provenance]].

## Suggested teaching activities (from-2021 SoW pp.23–24 — superseded, still usable)

- **Foraging exercise** — students gather online definitions and discussions of postmodernism independently, then the class tests and secures them together. Cambridge notes "there are plenty of definitions and discussions online," which for once makes independent research the efficient route.
- Test **pastiche, intertextuality and parody** against real media texts as characteristics emerge.
- Identify "what sorts of repertoires of knowledge and experience are necessary in order to make sense of certain media texts, and how they might function '**dialogically**' – that is, say one thing, but invoke other meanings through referencing or quoting from something else."
- Identify "ways in which belief in certain kinds of reality can be less important than belief in media representations of that reality."
- **Exercise:** "Watch a TV news story. What features does it have of 'The Society of the Spectacle'?"
- **Extension:** read up on the difference between pastiche and parody, then find contemporary examples of each.

Students already live in postmodern media — memes, remixes, franchise self-reference. Start from their examples, then supply the names.

## Technique note

> "As always, **anchoring the arguments in specific texts or phenomena is key** to demonstrating a sophisticated understanding of the concept and how it might be useful in making sense of contemporary media." (SoW p.24)

## ⚠ Who Cambridge does not name

Still absent from every document: **hyperreality, simulacra, Lyotard, metanarratives, Bakhtin, Hutcheon, Strinati.** "Dialogically" is used without crediting Bakhtin (carried into the 2028 edition unchanged), and intertextuality is never attributed at all. **Baudrillard** moved from wholly absent to a bare resource link in the 2028 SoW — his name now appears, his ideas still do not.

If a lesson teaches Baudrillard's concepts, that remains a centre choice — a defensible one given the syllabus's explicit pluralism (p.18), but it should be labeled as such.
