# Representation

**Key Concept 2.** "How the media construct the social world, the portrayal of ideas, individuals and groups" (Syllabus 2027 p.14).

This is the concept that turns description into argument. Component 2 Section A never asks you to list techniques — it asks how the extract *constructs* a person, group, event or place, and to what effect.

## The chain

The syllabus's logic runs:

**reality → selection and combination → mediation → a version**

Producers choose what is shown and how it is combined. Repeated choices harden into typicality. Typicality carries values. Values reinforced across many texts become ideology, and ideology positions audiences.

The Scheme of Work's glossary defines this hardening:

> "The processes (and end results) by which reality is subject to selection, exaggeration and repetition, so that certain representations (often of subordinated groups and associated places) become typical and taken for granted."

## What the syllabus requires

You study (Syllabus 2027 p.14):

- the ways in which events, issues, individuals and social groups are represented through processes of selection and combination
- the ways in which, through representation, versions of reality are mediated
- the processes which lead media producers to make choices about how to represent events, issues, individuals and social groups
- the effect of social and cultural context on representations
- how and why particular social groups, in a national and global context, may be under-represented or misrepresented
- how representations convey values, attitudes and beliefs about the world and how these may be systematically reinforced across a wide range of media
- how audiences respond to and interpret representations in the media
- the effect of historical context on representations
- how representations may invoke discourses and ideologies, and position audiences
- how interpretations of representations reflect social, cultural and historical circumstances

This is the longest content list of the four key concepts, and the only one that names **under-representation and misrepresentation** as objects of study in their own right.

Many of these bullets are about **context and change**: social, cultural, historical, national and global. Representation is not a fixed set of stereotypes; it shifts with who is making it, when, and for whom. This is why theories of the gaze are studied *across decades* rather than as settled fact.

## How it enters the exam

Component 2 Section A fuses representation with [[concepts/technical-elements|Technical Elements]]: you "discuss… how the following technical elements create specific representations of individuals/groups/events/places, and help to articulate specific messages and values that have social significance" (Syllabus 2027 p.16).

So the exam question is always some version of: **how is this person / group / place constructed, and to what effect?** An answer that names techniques without reaching representation has answered half the question.

The reassembly stage of [[concepts/the-viewing-protocol|The Viewing Protocol]] is where you practise this handoff: "How are particular representations of individuals, groups, events and places constructed through the technical elements identified?" (SoW p.14).

## Exercises you will do

- **Compare the coverage:** "Find two or three news stories about refugees/asylum seekers. How are they represented? What rhetoric is used? How is the reader positioned in relation to the refugees?" What is selected, what is omitted, how the reader is positioned. This connects to the [[concepts/power-and-the-media|Power and the Media]] debate.
- **Magazine representation grid:** "To what extent does the image conform to conventional ways of portraying men, women and celebrities? How are ethnic, cultural and subcultural dimensions emphasised or not?"
- **Advertising and power:** "What kinds of representations of women can you find in advertising? How 'powerful' are they?"
- **Stills annotation:** frames annotated with technical terms *plus* the representation each choice constructs.

## Theories of representation

You study theories of representation to understand how class, age, gender, ethnicity and other groups are portrayed. The theorists taught in [[classes/media-studies/lesson-plans/9607-s1-lesson-09-the-gaze|L09]] are van Zoonen, bell hooks and Laura Mulvey (gender as performance, the three looks, the oppositional gaze).

Section A analysis works along four **axes of representation** — class, age, gender, ethnicity. Some texts "will deploy stereotypical representations of some of these demographic social groups" while others "will consciously seek to challenge stereotypes." You run each technical element through these axes: camera ("Who is shot from a high/low angle? Who gets the close-ups?"), sound ("How does vocal performance construct representation of class / age / gender / ethnicity?"), and mise-en-scène ("Who occupies the foreground? Who is marginalised spatially?").

Mise-en-scène work also includes genre: compare two drama extracts and ask "How does mise-en-scène reinforce genre conventions?", and re-design a scene across social realism, noir crime, teen melodrama and sci-fi dystopia.
