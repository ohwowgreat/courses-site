# Representation

**Key Concept 2.** "How the media construct the social world, the portrayal of ideas, individuals and groups" (Syllabus 2027 p.14).

The concept that turns description into argument. Component 2 Section A never asks students to list techniques — it asks how the extract *constructs* a person, group, event or place, and to what effect.

## The chain

The syllabus's own logic, compressed from the p.14 bullets:

**reality → selection and combination → mediation → a version**

Producers choose what is shown and how it is combined. Repeated choices harden into typicality. Typicality carries values. Values systematically reinforced across many texts become ideology, and ideology positions audiences.

The Scheme of Work's glossary makes the hardening explicit (SoW p.27):

> "The processes (and end results) by which reality is subject to selection, exaggeration and repetition, so that certain representations (often of subordinated groups and associated places) become typical and taken for granted."

## What the syllabus requires

Candidates study (Syllabus 2027 p.14):

- the ways in which events, issues, individuals and social groups are represented through processes of selection and combination
- the ways in which, through representation, versions of reality are mediated
- the processes which lead media producers to make choices about how to represent events, issues, individuals and social groups
- the effect of social and cultural context on representations
- how and why particular social groups, in a national and global context, may be under-represented or misrepresented
- how representations convey values, attitudes and beliefs about the world and how these may be systematically reinforced across a wide range of media
- how audiences respond to and interpret representations in the media
- the effect of historical context on representations
- how representations may invoke discourses and ideologies, and position audiences
- how interpretations of representations reflect social, cultural and historical circumstances

Ten bullets — the longest content list of the four key concepts, and the only one that names **under-representation and misrepresentation** as objects of study in their own right.

Note how many bullets are about **context and change**: social, cultural, historical, national and global. Representation is not taught as a fixed set of stereotypes; it is taught as something that shifts with who is making, when, and for whom. That is why the BNDS scheme tests theories of the gaze *across decades* rather than as settled fact.

## How it enters the exam

Component 2 Section A fuses representation with [[concepts/technical-elements]]: candidates "discuss… how the following technical elements create specific representations of individuals/groups/events/places, and help to articulate specific messages and values that have social significance" (Syllabus 2027 p.16).

So the exam question is always some version of: **how is this person / group / place constructed, and to what effect?** A script that names techniques without reaching representation has answered half the question.

The reassembly stage of [[concepts/the-viewing-protocol]] is where this handoff is practised: "How are particular representations of individuals, groups, events and places constructed through the technical elements identified?" (SoW p.14).

## Teaching moves in the source

- **Compare the coverage** (SoW p.24, in the Power topic but usable from S1): "Find two or three news stories about refugees/asylum seekers. How are they represented? What rhetoric is used? How is the reader positioned in relation to the refugees?" What is selected, what is omitted, how the reader is positioned. This exercise scales from AS representation work to the A2 [[concepts/power-and-the-media]] debate.
- **Magazine representation grid** (SoW p.9): "To what extent does the image conform to conventional ways of portraying men, women and celebrities? How are ethnic, cultural and subcultural dimensions emphasised or not?"
- **Advertising and power** (SoW p.25): "What kinds of representations of women can you find in advertising? How 'powerful' are they?"
- **Stills annotation** — frames annotated with technical terms *plus* the representation each choice constructs. Blog-ready, and rehearses both C1 research and Paper 2 Section A.

## ⚠ Gaze theory: partly Cambridge-endorsed since the 2028 Scheme of Work

**Status revised 2026-07-20.** The claim used to be that no gaze theorist appears in any Cambridge document. The 2028 SoW changed this:

> "candidates need to develop the habit of applying knowledge of technical elements to better understand the representation of **class, age, gender, ethnicity**… candidates need to learn the concept of Representation - best done with reference to theories of representation where there is a variety of student-friendly materials available - such as **Hall, Van Zoonen, hooks, Gilroy, Alvarado**." (2028 SoW, C2 Section A)

So of the three theorists [[classes/media-studies/lesson-plans/9607-s1-lesson-09-the-gaze|L09]] teaches: **van Zoonen and bell hooks are now Cambridge-suggested** (surnames only, no ideas attributed); **Laura Mulvey remains a BNDS addition**, named in no Cambridge document. [[entities/paul-gilroy]] and [[entities/manuel-alvarado]] are newly named by Cambridge and not yet taught at BNDS — candidates for deepening the ethnicity strand in G12.

The labeling discipline still applies: a suggestion is not a prescription, and the *ideas* (gender as performance, the three looks, the oppositional gaze) are still attributed by nobody but us. When a student cites Mulvey, they are meeting the syllabus's *theory* requirement with a *centre-chosen* theorist. See [[analyses/9607-theory-provenance]].

The 2028 SoW also names the **axes of representation** for Section A analysis — class, age, gender, ethnicity — and states that texts "will deploy stereotypical representations of some of these demographic social groups and also that other texts will consciously seek to challenge stereotypes." Its exercise bank runs every technical element through those axes (camera: "Who is shot from a high/low angle? Who gets the close-ups?"; sound: "How does vocal performance construct representation of class / age / gender / ethnicity?"; mise-en-scène: "Who occupies the foreground? Who is marginalised spatially?").

## What Cambridge does not say

- **No representation theorist is named in either syllabus** — the five 2028 names live in the SoW's suggested column, without a single attributed idea.
- **"Stereotype" was not a Cambridge teaching term until the 2028 SoW**, which now uses "stereotypical representations" and "challenge stereotypes" freely. The syllabus itself still describes the process in longer form — representations "become typical and taken for granted" (SoW glossary), under-representation, misrepresentation.
- The from-2021 SoW's Component 2 section contains **no genre content at all**, despite genre being a Language bullet. The 2028 SoW partially fills this: its mise-en-scène bank includes a genre-deconstruction exercise (compare two drama extracts; "How does mise-en-scène reinforce genre conventions?") and a re-design task shifting a scene across social realism / noir crime / teen melodrama / sci-fi dystopia.
