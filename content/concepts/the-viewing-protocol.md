# The Viewing Protocol

The Scheme of Work's core analytical method, and the one worth ritualizing from week one: **break an extract into its component parts by isolating one element at a time, then reassemble it.**

Stated rationale (SoW p.13):

> "The principle here is to break down an extract from a TV drama into its component parts and then reassemble them in order to reach some insights into how it produces particular meanings."

⚠ **Status under the 2028 Scheme of Work (ingested 2026-07-20).** The break-down-and-reassemble principle survives — the 2028 edition's Section A introduction states it verbatim. But the **structured four-pass sequence below is gone**: the 2028 SoW replaces the single scaffolded protocol with large per-element exercise banks (shot-type drills, freeze-frame deconstruction, sound audits, mise-en-scène audits, editing timelines — see [[concepts/technical-elements]]). The isolation *move* survives only in the sound section: "play the extract with sound muted and again with sound on but with the screen off." The four-pass ritual is therefore now a **from-2021 method kept by BNDS choice** — still pedagogically sound, still matched to the exam's multiple screenings, but no longer Cambridge's presented sequence.

The 2028 edition also drops the *Handmaid's Tale* example and adds extract-selection guidance instead: extracts "within 5 years of the teaching of each cohort," from different organisations and a mix of genres — see [[concepts/case-study-method]].

## The four passes

Verbatim from SoW p.13:

> "Start by scaffolding the process – show a five-minute opening and explain that no response is required yet. Then show it with the sound down and invite comments on visual elements – mise-en-scène first, perhaps. Then again (still with the sound off) and elicit observations about camera shots, angles, movement and composition. Then focus on sound by covering or blanking the screen"

| Pass | What runs | What students read |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Full extract, ~5 min | Nothing — "no response is required yet" |
| 2 | Sound down | Mise-en-scène |
| 3 | Sound still off | Camera: shots, angles, movement, composition |
| 4 | Screen covered or blanked | Sound |

The sound pass carries its own prompts (SoW p.13): "What do we hear? / Is it diegetic or non-diegetic? / What mood is created? / How could the music be described? / To whom are the voices addressed?"

## The editing pass

Editing gets its own move, because it is the element students cannot see (SoW p.13):

> "A focus on editing brings many of these elements together – initially it might be useful for learners to count out loud, or tap whenever there is a visual transition, which is a simple exercise, but it makes something potentially 'invisible' become visible."

Then the follow-up questions (SoW pp.13–14): "Is it a cut or a mix/dissolve? / What's the effect of this? / What visual elements are brought together? / Does it create continuity? / Contrast? / Does it reveal something about character? / Does it develop the story?"

## The reassembly

The protocol is not finished until the parts go back together (SoW p.14):

> "Finally, bring all these elements together:
> - What is the function of this opening?
> - How does it set up the beginning of a story?
> - How does it establish character, mood and setting?
> - What kinds of competence does it require of the audience?
> - How are particular representations of individuals, groups, events and places constructed through the technical elements identified?"

The last question is the one that converts description into exam marks. It is where the protocol hands off to [[concepts/representation]].

## Choosing the extract

The Scheme of Work is specific (SoW p.13):

- "Openings of first episodes usually work well, because they establish setting, tone, character, and set up some narrative expectations."
- "Select a range of examples (you could use ones which have previously featured in examinations, such as **The Handmaid's Tale**) – the development of expertise in what to look for and how to articulate it is an iterative process."

Using past exam extracts makes the practice authentic and calibrates students to the real difficulty level.

## Output

"Learners should practise the analytical process, possibly in groups and then present their findings, and/or individually in a short-form essay. (F)" (SoW p.14). The **(F)** marks it as formative — group analysis presented, then individual short-form writing, frequent and low stakes.

## Why it is worth the ritual

Three reasons, all defensible from the source:

1. It is the **direct rehearsal for Component 2 Section A**, where the extract is screened four times (Syllabus 2027 p.23). Four screenings is not generosity — it is the exam's own version of this protocol, and students who have practised element-isolation know what to do with each pass.
2. It **rescues sound**, which the Scheme of Work names as the neglected element (SoW p.13). A dedicated screen-covered pass is the only reliable way to make students hear.
3. It **continues from C1**: "This work could build on analytical work for the Foundation Portfolio, as the approaches and terminology are the same" (SoW p.13). The same protocol serves research for the portfolio and analysis for the exam.
