# The Viewing Protocol

The course's core analytical method, worth building into a routine from week one: **break an extract into its component parts by isolating one element at a time, then reassemble it.**

> "The principle here is to break down an extract from a TV drama into its component parts and then reassemble them in order to reach some insights into how it produces particular meanings."

You will also work through per-element exercises — shot-type drills, freeze-frame deconstruction, sound audits, mise-en-scène audits, editing timelines (see [[concepts/technical-elements]]). Extracts are chosen from within about five years of when you study them, from different organisations and across a mix of genres (see [[concepts/case-study-method]]).

## The four passes

| Pass | What runs | What you focus on |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Full extract, ~5 min | Nothing — no response is required yet |
| 2 | Sound down | Mise-en-scène |
| 3 | Sound still off | Camera: shots, angles, movement, composition |
| 4 | Screen covered or blanked | Sound |

The sound pass has its own prompts: What do we hear? Is it diegetic or non-diegetic? What mood is created? How could the music be described? To whom are the voices addressed?

## The editing pass

Editing gets its own move, because it is the element you cannot see:

> "A focus on editing brings many of these elements together – initially it might be useful for learners to count out loud, or tap whenever there is a visual transition, which is a simple exercise, but it makes something potentially 'invisible' become visible."

Follow-up questions: Is it a cut or a mix/dissolve? What's the effect of this? What visual elements are brought together? Does it create continuity? Contrast? Does it reveal something about character? Does it develop the story?

## The reassembly

The protocol is not finished until the parts go back together:

> - What is the function of this opening?
> - How does it set up the beginning of a story?
> - How does it establish character, mood and setting?
> - What kinds of competence does it require of the audience?
> - How are particular representations of individuals, groups, events and places constructed through the technical elements identified?

The last question is the one that converts description into exam marks. It is where the protocol hands off to [[concepts/representation]].

## Choosing the extract

- Openings of first episodes usually work well, because they establish setting, tone, character, and set up some narrative expectations.
- A range of examples is used, including ones that have previously featured in examinations. Building expertise in what to look for and how to articulate it is an iterative process.

Using past exam extracts makes the practice authentic and calibrates you to the real difficulty level.

## Output

You practise the analytical process, possibly in groups and then presenting your findings, and/or individually in a short-form essay. This is formative **(F)** — frequent and low stakes.

## Why it is worth the routine

1. It is direct rehearsal for **Component 2 Section A**, where the extract is screened four times. Four screenings is the exam's own version of this protocol, and if you have practised element-isolation you know what to do with each pass.
2. It **rescues sound**, the element most easily neglected. A dedicated screen-covered pass is the most reliable way to make yourself hear.
3. It **continues from C1**: the approaches and terminology are the same, so the protocol serves both portfolio research and exam analysis.
