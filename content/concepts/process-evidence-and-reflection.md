# Process Evidence and Reflection

How a candidate proves they made the thing, and what they say about it afterwards. Two of the five C1 marking criteria attach directly to this material, and the evidence layer is the part of the coursework that most often caps an otherwise strong product.

⚠ **This is the page most affected by the syllabus straddle.** G11 sits the 2027 spec (blog + creative critical reflection). G12 sits the 2028–30 spec (slide portfolio + ~2000-word evaluative essay). Everything below is the **2027/from-2021 regime** unless marked. See [[classes/media-studies/media-studies]] for the crossover.

## The blog (2027 spec)

**Required, individually, from the start of research.** "Learners should set up and maintain a blog outlining the creative processes… The blog should be **set up at the outset of the research stage** and should be **maintained regularly**" (CWH p.6). Group members each keep their own: "All learners are required to maintain **separate blogs**… These should include details of their individual role within the group task" (CWH p.6).

### What must be in it (Syllabus 2027 p.21)

- the process of research, planning and production
- refinement, changes or edits and reflections on key moments
- all individual contributions to group tasks
- evidence of any preliminary exercises
- the final finished product, clearly labeled
- the creative critical reflection, clearly labelled

Plus the coverage rule: students "should ensure that they engage with the key areas: **forms and conventions, production contexts, the role of technologies, audiences and representations**" (CWH p.7).

The Coursework Handbook gives the process chain the blog should narrate (CWH p.9): set up the blog → determine groupings and select the brief → research codes and conventions → storyboarding, casting, scripting, recce reports, risk assessments → carry out production → reflect and edit → evaluate in light of the contemporary media landscape.

### Dating and freezing

"Learners should aim to update their blog regularly in order to ensure a **continuous narrative of the production process**" (CWH p.10). No minimum post count or frequency exists anywhere in either document — "regularly" is as specific as Cambridge gets.

But the freeze rule is hard: "**Blogs must be accessible online for the duration of the moderation period. During this period no changes may be made to the portfolios**" (CWH p.21). Centres present one hub URL linking every candidate's blog, recorded on the Individual Candidate Record Card (CWH pp.10, 20).

This is why the BNDS rule is *posts dated the day they happen, never backfilled* — a blog assembled retrospectively cannot show a continuous narrative, and there is no opportunity to fix it after submission.

### Why a thin blog caps a good product

Research and planning are criterion 4 of the five C1 criteria, and the mark scheme judges the blog, the product and the reflection **together at every level** (CWH pp.22–23). Level 5 requires research "drawn on to inform the production and explored in the creative critical reflection"; Level 2 describes a blog that "may make reference to the research but may not fully engage with it." A strong product with a thin blog cannot reach the top band, because the evidence for the top band lives in the blog.

## Group work

Maximum **four** candidates (Syllabus 2027 pp.20, 21; CWH p.6), with "clearly defined roles."

Cambridge's advice on forming groups (CWH p.10): consider skill set rather than friendship; "**Each learner in the group should adopt an area of expertise. This does not necessarily mean that they are the only person to work on this element, but that they lead on that part of the production.**" Suggested roles — director, technical, creative, editor — are examples, not a required set.

Authentication tightens for groups: "Sufficient work must be carried out under **direct supervision** to enable you to authenticate the coursework marks, with confidence. This is particularly important if the production work is carried out as a group" (CWH p.8), and the teacher must comment on the individual's own contribution on the record card.

For the magazine option under the 2027 spec, group members each produce **a unique edition in the same house style**, with "a minimum of four images per candidate" (Syllabus 2027 p.21).

## The creative critical reflection (C1, 2027 spec)

Individual, in a digital format of the candidate's choice — "any digital format, such as director commentaries, a presentation with voiceover, podcasts or screencasts," and a **different format may be used per question** (Syllabus 2027 p.22).

### The four compulsory questions (Syllabus 2027 p.22)

1. "How does your product use or challenge conventions and how does it represent social groups or issues?"
2. "How does your product engage with audiences and how would it be distributed as a real media text?"
3. "How did your production skills develop throughout this project?"
4. "How did you integrate technologies – software, hardware and online – in this project?"

Note the shape: Q1 is Language + Representation, Q2 is Audience + Industry, Q3 and Q4 are AO3 process. The four questions are the [[concepts/four-key-concepts]] wearing a different hat.

The CCR must also state "the role they adopted if working as part of a group" and "draw on evidence from the research, production and editing stages" (CWH p.13). It sits **before the final post** in the blog (CWH p.13).

### The failure mode Cambridge names

> "there is often a tendency for learners to provide a **blow by blow account** of the production process instead of a genuinely critical evaluation of the final work and how it was developed. One way round this is to ensure that there is a **focus on critical incidents and turning points**" (SoW p.10, repeated p.12)

The prompts that produce turning points: "What were the breakthroughs in developing a story opening? How were particular technical challenges overcome? What new understanding was gained at key points in the process? **Is the final product successful? According to what criteria?**"

That last question is the one most students never answer, and it is where AO2 marks live.

**No word count or duration exists for the C1 CCR.** The 1000–1500 word figure belongs to C3, not C1.

## The C3 critical reflection (2027 spec)

"An evaluative essay of around 1000–1500 words" (Syllabus 2027 p.25), individual "even if learners had collaborated in the creation of their products" (CWH p.7), guided by four compulsory questions:

1. "How do your products represent social groups or issues?"
2. "How do the elements of your production work together to create a sense of 'branding'?"
3. "How do your products engage with the audience?"
4. "How did your research inform your products and the way they use or challenge conventions?"

⚠ **Erratum already logged in the vault:** the syllabus's assessment overview (p.10) says "around 1000 words" while the detail section (p.25) says "around 1000–1500 words." Two figures, one document. Recorded as flag 8 on [[classes/media-studies/media-studies]].

## How it is marked (C1, 2027 spec)

Five criteria, 10 marks each, 50 total (Syllabus 2027 p.31; CWH pp.22–23):

| Criterion | AO |
|---|---|
| Analyse and evaluate own work | **AO2** (10 marks) |
| Use of media language | AO3 |
| Use of technical skills | AO3 |
| Research, planning and application of key concepts | AO3 |
| Creative Critical Reflection skills | AO3 |

**AO3 Practical Application = 40 marks; AO2 Evaluation = 10 marks** (Syllabus 2027 p.31).

Evidence sources per AO (CWH p.21): AO2 is evidenced **solely** by the reflection; AO3 is evidenced by the blog, the finished product, and — at AS only — the reflection as well. So the CCR feeds both objectives at AS. Marking is best-fit against generic grids, with part marks rounded up.

**AO1 is not assessed in the C1 grid at all** — though the Scheme of Work tags AO1 against C1 teaching rows (SoW p.7). Taught, not marked, at this component.

## What changes for G12 (2028–30 spec)

Per [[classes/media-studies/media-studies]] and the 2028–30 syllabus: evidence moves from an online blog to a **dated chronological slide portfolio** on Submit for Assessment; the reflection becomes a single **~2000-word evaluative essay**; the coursework AO split changes from AO2 20 / AO3 80 to **AO1 20 / AO2 20 / AO3 60**; C3's third medium becomes a **website home page**; and the C1 magazine task becomes **individual only**.

The *habits* transfer — dated evidence, decisions traceable to research, turning points over blow-by-blow. The *format* does not. Do not let G12 students carry blog-shaped assumptions into a slide portfolio.

## ⚠ The 2028 Scheme of Work contradicts its own syllabus here (found 2026-07-20)

The **2028 Scheme of Work's C1 and C3 sections are near-verbatim carryovers of the from-2021 text and still describe the old evidence regime**: "recorded in the learner's individual blog" (both C1 briefs and C3), "a critical reflection" answering the four compulsory questions, and C3 minor tasks including "a **social media page**" (film promotion and documentary options). Nowhere does the 2028 SoW mention the slide portfolio, Submit for Assessment, the ~2000-word evaluative essay, or the website home page.

**On every one of these points the 2028–30 syllabus is the authority and the 2028 SoW is stale.** The syllabus prescribes the slide portfolio, the essay, and the website home page; the SoW is Cambridge's suggested-activities document and its process language was simply not revised. Treat the 2028 SoW's *activities* as usable (research, practice exercises, pitching, the turning-points reflection discipline — all format-independent) and its *evidence and submission wording* as superseded. Do not cite the 2028 SoW for anything about how coursework is evidenced or reflected on.

Recorded as flag 9 on [[classes/media-studies/media-studies]]. The failure-mode advice ("blow by blow account… focus on critical incidents and turning points") appears unchanged in the 2028 SoW — that advice transfers cleanly to the evaluative essay.
