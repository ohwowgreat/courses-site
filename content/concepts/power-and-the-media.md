# Power and the Media

One of the three **A Level Media debates** topics (Syllabus 2027 pp.9, 18); the second of the two BNDS has provisionally chosen, alongside [[concepts/postmodern-media]].

Syllabus framing: "Power and the media considers issues surrounding access to the media, identity, representation, ideology and democracy" (p.18).

The core question: **who gets to speak, who gets spoken for, and whose version of the world wins?**

## What candidates must study (Syllabus 2027 p.18)

- the relationships between media texts, their producers, distributors and audiences
- the mediation of national, regional, individual and group identities
- the dynamic nature of power relationships within and between audiences and the media
- the power of rhetoric in the media
- the construction and reception of campaigning and marketing messages
- the way access to the media may be limited or controlled, including the democratic distribution of voices

The Scheme of Work reproduces the last five as "Key areas in the examination are" (SoW p.25).

⚠ Unlike the other two debates, **this topic has no "arguments for and against" bullet.** It is framed as interrogation rather than adjudication — students analyze how power operates, rather than arguing whether it should.

## Three kinds of power

The Scheme of Work's framing implies a useful taxonomy (SoW p.24):

> "This could be **the power to represent, to speak for a particular group**, or the power to **acquire independent companies in order to benefit from their brand and market share**. It could also be the growing **power of individuals to produce and distribute their own material**, to create their own brands and to make an impact on political discourse via social media."

Representational power · corporate power · distributed individual power. Most good essays on this topic set at least two of them against each other.

## Dominant ideology

> "although there may now be an enormous quantity of voices in the public sphere, there is still a sense that some voices are more powerful than others, some representations are more potent than others, and that **when media messages are aligned with dominant ideologies they are particularly powerful**." (SoW p.25)

This connects the topic straight back to [[concepts/representation]] — the systematic reinforcement of values across many texts is the mechanism by which representation becomes power.

## The named thinker

**[[entities/slavoj-zizek]]** is the only theorist Cambridge names here (SoW p.25):

> "Media ownership will be one factor to consider, and there are examples of media outlets being directly influenced, or self-censoring, because of their owners, but more significant will be what **Slavoj Žižek** calls the media's **'Standard Operating Procedure'** – its reproduction of ideological 'truths' about which voices count for more than others, its reduction of complex conflicts to spectacle, and the general reinforcing of 'common sense'."

## Counter-power

> "Learners might also consider, however, how media messages may be rejected or subverted, perhaps using strategies of **détournement**, producing their own texts, or mounting social media campaigns." (SoW p.25)

Cambridge's example is a Guardian piece on young Brixton activists recreating film posters with Black leads. Note that **détournement is named without attribution** — and, oddly, without connecting it back to [[entities/guy-debord]] and the Situationists, despite Debord appearing two pages earlier in [[concepts/postmodern-media]]. That link is worth making in class; it is not made in the source.

## What the 2028 Scheme of Work changes (ingested 2026-07-20)

The framing text — the three kinds of power, the Žižek passage, détournement (still unattributed) — carries over. Two changes matter:

**The politically hottest exercises are gone.** The Brexit/Trump campaign exercise and the Lenny Henry diversity question do not appear in the 2028 edition. What survives from the old set: the refugee/asylum-seeker news-story exercise and the women-in-advertising exercise. One line is added after the Žižek passage: "In the years since the publication of this article, it is now also worth considering how much has changed in terms of increased representation of different ethnicities in media productions - and also analyse how and why degrees of change have occurred."

**A structured activity bank arrives**, one exercise per syllabus bullet: media power flow analysis (producers → distributors → platforms → audiences; gatekeeping, concentration) · mediation of national/regional/group/individual identity · audience power dynamics (activism, backlash, fan campaigns; "whether participatory culture increases or commodifies audience power") · rhetorical strategy analysis (framing, emotive language, binary oppositions) · campaign analysis (mode of address, ideological positioning, oppositional readings) · access mapping (ownership concentration, economic barriers, **platform algorithms**, cultural gatekeeping) · a synoptic debate on the motion "Media power remains concentrated in the hands of institutions rather than audiences."

New resource links: **Media Reform Coalition, Ofcom media-ownership rules, Pew, Reuters Institute Digital News Report** — the first named institutional sources this topic has ever had.

## Suggested exercises (from-2021 SoW pp.24–25 — partly superseded)

1. "Find two or three news stories about refugees/asylum seekers. How are they represented? What rhetoric is used? How is the reader positioned in relation to the refugees?" *(retained in 2028)*
2. "Examine the 'Brexit' campaign in the UK and/or Donald Trump's election campaign in the USA. What kinds of power in the media were evident?" *(dropped in 2028)*
3. "What does Lennie Henry's campaign about diversity on television tell us about power in the media?" *(the source spells the forename both "Lennie" and "lenny"; the subject is Sir Lenny Henry — dropped in 2028)*
4. "What kinds of representations of women can you find in advertising? How 'powerful' are they?" *(retained in 2028)*

## ⚠ No sensitivity advice is given for this topic

Worth stating plainly, because the asymmetry is real: [[concepts/media-regulation]] carries two explicit warnings about handling the subject sensitively by region (SoW p.22, retained in 2028). **Power and the media carries none, in either edition** — though the 2028 revision quietly removed its most nationally specific exercises (Brexit, Trump, Lenny Henry), which reads as the same concern acted on without being stated.

The remaining gap is smaller but real: the refugee exercise survives. For a BNDS cohort the exercises need local substitution or reframing, chosen deliberately rather than by default. The syllabus content itself — rhetoric, campaigning messages, access, the democratic distribution of voices — is fully teachable through examples that do not import a specific national politics.

## ⚠ Who Cambridge does not name

Absent: **Gramsci, hegemony, Stuart Hall, Habermas, Herman & Chomsky, the propaganda model, Curran & Seaton, Foucault.** "Public sphere," "dominant ideologies" and "common sense" all appear as bare vocabulary with no originator credited. Žižek is the sole named thinker in the topic. See [[analyses/9607-theory-provenance]].
