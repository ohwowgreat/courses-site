# Four Key Concepts

The spine of CIE 9607. Everything examined in the course is an application of one of four ideas: **Language**, **Representation**, **Industry**, **Audience**. The syllabus states them once as a list and again, at length, as subject content — you "should be prepared to demonstrate their knowledge and understanding of these across all components" (Syllabus 2027 p.14).

You are taught these as a set in [[classes/media-studies/lesson-plans/9607-s1-lesson-01-the-four-key-concepts|L01]], then rehearse them for the rest of the course.

## The syllabus definitions

| | Definition (Syllabus 2027 p.5) | Page for detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Language** | "How the media communicate meanings through their forms, codes, conventions and techniques" | [[concepts/media-language]] |
| **Representation** | "How the media construct the social world including the portrayal of ideas, individuals and groups" | [[concepts/representation]] |
| **Industry** | "How and why media texts are produced, distributed and circulated" | [[concepts/media-industry]] |
| **Audience** | "How audiences are constructed and addressed by media texts and how audiences interpret and respond to media texts" | [[concepts/media-audience]] |

The Scheme of Work uses the same four definitions and keys the whole document to them as KC1–KC4. Where a scheme-of-work row is tagged KC3, it is teaching Industry.

You are assessed on your **direct knowledge and understanding** of the four concepts. You should be able to **describe and explain** them, as well as **apply them to novel situations and evaluate them**.

Each component carries 90 guided learning hours / 25% of the course. The suggested teaching order is C1 → C2 → C3 → C4.

Two things about the wording are worth holding onto:

- **Audience is the only two-clause definition.** It covers both how audiences are *constructed and addressed* (the producer's side) and how they *interpret and respond* (the audience's side). The other three are single-clause. This is why audience work splits into industry-facing targeting and reception-facing interpretation.
- **Industry is defined twice, differently.** The key-concepts page says "produced, distributed and **circulated**" (p.5); the subject content says "produced, distributed and **consumed**" (pp.14–15). Both are the syllabus's own words. Cite whichever page you are quoting; do not blend them.

## Where each concept is heaviest

| Component | Concepts tagged |
|---|---|
| C1 Foundation Portfolio | all four, applied to your own product |
| C2 Section A (unseen extract) | KC2 Representation, KC3 Industry, KC4 Audience (SoW p.13) |
| C2 Section B (contexts) | all four (SoW p.14) |
| C3 Advanced Portfolio | all four (SoW p.17) |
| C4 Section A (the three debates) | KC1 Language, KC2 Representation only (SoW pp.22, 23, 24) |
| C4 Section B (Media ecology) | all four, synoptically (SoW p.25) |

C2 Section A is in practice the most language-heavy task in the course.

The C4 Section A focus on Language and Representation only is a real structural signal: the three debates are argued about how the media *speak* and *portray*, while Industry and Audience are carried by the synoptic Section B.

## What the concepts are not

They are not four separate topics taught in sequence and then closed. The syllabus builds them into one another — the Language content includes "how audiences respond to and interpret media language" (p.14), and the Representation content includes "how audiences respond to and interpret representations" (p.15).

Cambridge also invites additions: "You may identify additional key concepts which will also enrich teaching and learning" (p.5). A centre may teach, say, narrative or ideology as a fifth spine — but only these four are assessed as such.

## Capitalization

The syllabus capitalizes the concepts as **Language, Representation, Industry, Audience** on pp.5, 9 and 14, but C4 Section B (p.26) writes them lower case, and the portfolio skills list (p.14) uses a different noun set: "media language, representation, media **industries** and **audiences**."
