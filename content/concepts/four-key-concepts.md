# Four Key Concepts

The spine of CIE 9607. Everything examined in the course is an application of one of four ideas: **Language**, **Representation**, **Industry**, **Audience**. The syllabus states them once as a list and again, at length, as subject content — candidates "should be prepared to demonstrate their knowledge and understanding of these across all components" (Syllabus 2027 p.14).

Taught as a set in [[classes/media-studies/lesson-plans/9607-s1-lesson-01-the-four-key-concepts|L01]], then rehearsed for the rest of the course.

## The syllabus definitions

| | Definition (Syllabus 2027 p.5) | Page for detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Language** | "How the media communicate meanings through their forms, codes, conventions and techniques" | [[concepts/media-language]] |
| **Representation** | "How the media construct the social world including the portrayal of ideas, individuals and groups" | [[concepts/representation]] |
| **Industry** | "How and why media texts are produced, distributed and circulated" | [[concepts/media-industry]] |
| **Audience** | "How audiences are constructed and addressed by media texts and how audiences interpret and respond to media texts" | [[concepts/media-audience]] |

The Scheme of Work reproduces these four definitions word for word and keys the whole document to them as KC1–KC4 (SoW p.4). Where a scheme-of-work row is tagged KC3, it is teaching Industry.

**The 2028 Scheme of Work sharpens the assessment claim** (ingested 2026-07-20). Its introduction states: "learners will be assessed on their **direct knowledge and understanding** of the same. Learners should be able to **describe and explain** the key concepts as well as demonstrate their ability to **apply them to novel situations and evaluate them**." The from-2021 edition merely said the concepts were "highlighted as a separate item in the new syllabus." Describe → explain → apply-to-novel-situations → evaluate is a usable progression for building CS tasks, and the "novel situations" phrase is the strongest internal justification for cold-read practice.

The 2028 introduction also adds a **guided-learning-hours table**: 90 hours / 25% of the course per component, suggested teaching order C1 → C2 → C3 → C4. (The table mislabels Components 3 and 4 as "Component 1" — an obvious typo, noted so nobody cites it as printed.)

Two things about the wording are worth holding onto:

- **Audience is the only two-clause definition.** It covers both how audiences are *constructed and addressed* (the producer's side) and how they *interpret and respond* (the audience's side). The other three are single-clause. That asymmetry is the reason audience work splits into industry-facing targeting and reception-facing interpretation.
- **Industry is defined twice, differently.** The key-concepts page says "produced, distributed and **circulated**" (p.5); the subject content says "produced, distributed and **consumed**" (pp.14–15). Both are the syllabus's own words. Cite whichever page you are quoting; do not blend them.

## Where each concept is heaviest

Derived from the KC tagging in the Scheme of Work and the component structure in the syllabus:

| Component | Concepts tagged |
|---|---|
| C1 Foundation Portfolio | all four, applied to the candidate's own product |
| C2 Section A (unseen extract) | KC2 Representation, KC3 Industry, KC4 Audience (SoW p.13) — see note below |
| C2 Section B (contexts) | all four (SoW p.14) |
| C3 Advanced Portfolio | all four (SoW p.17) |
| C4 Section A (the three debates) | KC1 Language, KC2 Representation only (SoW pp.22, 23, 24) |
| C4 Section B (Media ecology) | all four, synoptically (SoW p.25) |

⚠ **Uncertain:** KC1 does not appear in the Section A tag column of the extracted Scheme of Work text. Given how the PDF's three-column table extracts, it may have been lost to layout rather than genuinely omitted — Section A is in practice the most language-heavy task in the course. Do not cite the omission as fact without checking the printed page.

The C4 Section A tagging (Language and Representation only) is a real structural signal, not an accident: the three debates are argued about how the media *speak* and *portray*, while Industry and Audience are carried by the synoptic Section B.

## What the concepts are not

They are not four separate topics to be taught in sequence and then closed. The syllabus builds them into one another — the Language content includes "how audiences respond to and interpret media language" (p.14), and the Representation content includes "how audiences respond to and interpret representations" (p.15). A lesson that teaches Language without reaching Audience is teaching half the bullet.

Cambridge also invites additions: "You may identify additional key concepts which will also enrich teaching and learning" (p.5). Nothing stops a centre teaching, say, narrative or ideology as a fifth spine — but only these four are assessed as such.

## Capitalization trap

The syllabus capitalizes the concepts as **Language, Representation, Industry, Audience** on pp.5, 9 and 14 — but C4 Section B (p.26) writes them lower case, and the portfolio skills list (p.14) uses a different noun set entirely: "media language, representation, media **industries** and **audiences**." The wiki uses the capitalized forms for the concepts and the lower-case forms for the general nouns.
