# Media Ecology

**Compulsory** A Level content — the only Component 4 topic with no choice attached (Syllabus 2027 pp.9, 19). Examined as Component 4 Section B: one 30-mark synoptic question, half the paper.

Renamed **Changing media environments** in the 2028–30 syllabus, which is the spec G12 sits. Content transfers; the name and the exam format do not. See [[classes/media-studies/media-studies]].

> ⚠ **Two generations of teaching support now exist for this topic, and they differ substantially.** Sections below marked "(from-2021 SoW)" describe the old support; the "What the 2028 Scheme of Work replaces" section describes the new. **G12 sits the 2028–30 spec, so the 2028 SoW's version is the one to plan from**; the from-2021 material is kept because several of its moves (the founders' genealogy, the before-1990 exercise, dystopian thought experiments) remain usable centre choices.

## The syllabus definition (p.19)

> "Media ecology refers to the complex environments within which media texts, producers, distributors, technologies and audiences exist. It is a way of conceiving the structure of these environments, their content, and impact on people."

## Possible areas of focus (Syllabus 2027 p.19)

- audience engagement with evolving media environments
- convergence of personal communication technology and mass communication technology
- impact on society of technological change including the collection and sharing of information and data protection
- the nature of globalisation and the responses of audiences and institutions
- the changing nature of media ownership and distribution models including net neutrality
- the representation of public and private personae
- the relationships between software, hardware and audiences
- the impact of developing technologies on media language
- changing modes of reception and their impact on audiences

⚠ **This is the only topic in the syllabus phrased as "Possible areas of focus for candidates are" rather than "Candidates should study,"** and it closes with: "The possible areas of focus are not exhaustive but provide examples of how candidates might adapt and apply their knowledge and understanding gained throughout the course" (p.19).

Media ecology is therefore a **compulsory topic with non-compulsory sub-content**. Nothing on that list has to be taught; something like it does.

## What "synoptic" means here

The exam requirement (Syllabus 2027 p.26):

> "Section B is synoptic, requiring candidates to **synthesise their knowledge and understanding of the key concepts of language, representation, industry and audience, studied throughout the course**. Candidates are expected to adapt and apply this knowledge and understanding to contemporary examples from **at least two media forms** in order to explore evolving media environments."

The two-media-form requirement is stated twice in the same paragraph. It is the hardest constraint in Component 4 — and, per [[concepts/case-study-method]], studying one media *area* at AS does not satisfy it.

The Scheme of Work adds two learning objectives unique to this topic (SoW p.26): "**Synthesising** knowledge and understanding of the key concepts" and "**Adapting and applying** knowledge of key concepts to a variety of media environments." No other Component 4 topic gets either verb.

And the strongest synoptic instruction in the document: "Examples from other components **can and should**, be drawn upon – particularly those that illustrate changes in power, technology and audience engagement" (SoW p.26).

**Nothing new is taught here so much as everything already taught is re-aimed.**

## What the 2028 Scheme of Work replaces (ingested 2026-07-20)

The 2028 SoW rebuilds this topic end to end under the new name. The load-bearing changes:

**The founders are gone.** No McLuhan, no Postman, no genealogy sentence — no thinker of any kind is named. See [[entities/marshall-mcluhan]], [[entities/neil-postman]], [[analyses/9607-theory-provenance]].

**The media diary moved here.** The one-day diary exercise that the from-2021 SoW placed in Component 2 Section B (with its seven expected insights, all carried over verbatim, plus the attention-economy extension) now opens this topic directly. The extension updates its platform list: "how I use Facebook" becomes "Facebook / X / BlueSky / Snapchat / Instagram."

**A three-era periodization replaces "before 1990."** Audience behaviour is compared across the **broadcast era** (to the late 1980s — "launch of Sky in the UK"), the **early digital era** (late 1980s–early 2000s — "to the arrival of Netflix in the UK and the launch of BBC iPlayer"), and the **contemporary platform era** (2000s onwards). Note the UK-anchored boundaries; for the BNDS cohort the eras translate naturally (CCTV-dominant broadcast era → portal/BBS era → WeChat/Bilibili/Douyin platform era), but that translation is ours to make.

**Four structured focus areas** replace the old nine-bullet list, each with sub-bullets and a worked activity:

1. **Audience behaviour** — engagement with evolving environments; changing modes of reception; changes to "audiences' perception of knowledge, truth and reality"; fan and "prosumer" culture
2. **Economics of media** — ownership and distribution models; funding models; globalisation and the responses of audiences and institutions
3. **Ethics** — platform responsibility for what is "published"; technological change incl. data collection and protection; public and private personae
4. **Technological convergence** — old/new media blurring; personal + mass communication convergence; software/hardware/audience relationships; impact on media language, codes and conventions

Two old bullets do not survive the reorganization: **"net neutrality"** (dropped from the ownership bullet) — and nothing replaces it. New arrivals: **"fan and 'prosumer' culture"** and **perception of knowledge/truth/reality** are explicit for the first time.

**Synoptic links are now signposted per activity.** Each focus-area activity closes with named links to the Section A topics (e.g. Audience behaviour → "Power: Audience agency versus institutional control; Postmodern Media: Hyperreality and fragmented identity; Regulation: Challenges in moderating user-generated content"). The from-2021 SoW asserted the topic was synoptic; the 2028 SoW operationalizes it.

**Resource links exist now**: Nielsen Norman Group (attention economy), Pew Research Centre, Ofcom Online Nation Report. The from-2021 edition's ecology section cited none.

Dropped along with the founders: the dystopian/utopian thought experiments, the debate sessions, and the blog/vlog output formats.

## How the from-2021 Scheme of Work characterized it

Four framing statements, and they are the entire conceptual content Cambridge offers (SoW pp.25–26):

1. "As the concept of Media Ecology draws upon all Media forms and ideas it is by its very nature **synoptic** in relation to this course."
2. "The notion of media ecology can be traced back to **Neil Postman** and **Marshall McLuhan**, but its contemporary relevance is clearly driven by **the saturation of all aspects of contemporary existence with media and media technologies**."
3. "Work on this component is inevitably **historical – contrasting the present with the past, and analysing the present in order to develop ideas about the future**."
4. "learners should be encouraged to develop a broad perspective in order to understand how contemporary life is **'mediatised'**, that is, that we are always thinking and acting through and with mediated information and media technologies."

⚠ Note what is *missing*: **Cambridge never explains the ecology metaphor.** There is no "media as environments we live inside rather than tools we pick up" gloss anywhere in either document. What the source gives is a genealogy ([[entities/neil-postman]], [[entities/marshall-mcluhan]]), a temporal method, a saturation premise, and the word "mediatised." The environment metaphor is the teacher's to supply.

## The launch lesson (from-2021 SoW p.25 — superseded, still usable)

> "Learners could conduct a piece of **group-based historical research** in order to kick start this work: **'What did the media landscape look like before 1990?'** This should elicit insights into pre world-wide-web media – the limitations of distribution and access to media, the limited range of media forms, the physicality of media, the clear demarcations between media, the distinction between 'mass media' and personal communications."

Then contrast it with "learners' **descriptions/diaries of their own media habits**" — which is why the Grade 11 media diary from [[concepts/media-audience]] should be kept. The gap between the two columns is the content of the essay.

## Other suggested activities (from-2021 SoW p.26 — superseded, still usable)

- Examine "the ways in which their own language, thought and identity are already influenced and inflected by the media."
- **Dystopian and utopian fictions as thought experiments**, feeding "debate sessions in which provocative proposals are offered by one side and contested by the other." No specific texts are named.
- Use "contemporary evidence of technological development, ownership patterns, marketing, social media and media production trends" — this "will all equip candidates to engage with the **informed, imaginative analysis** needed for this component."
- Output in blog entries, presentations, handouts or vlogs.
- **Pair mind-map carousel** for exam practice (SoW p.26): "learners sit with one other person to mind map responses to a question before moving to a different question with a different partner. By approaching exam questions in this way learners feel supported and gain skills at responding quickly to previously unseen tasks."

## Plan for it in Grade 11

Cambridge says this twice, and it is the single most actionable planning note in the Scheme of Work:

> "Note also that the work for Component 2 lays the foundations for Component 4 – particularly the Media Ecology element" (SoW p.17)

The AS case study, the media diaries, and the industry work in [[classes/media-studies/unit-plans/9607-s1-unit-5-media-contexts|Unit 5]] are all Component 4 material with an eighteen-month delay. Choose and store them accordingly.

The 2028 SoW keeps this pointer — amusingly, in its old wording: its Component 3 introduction still reads "particularly the **Media Ecology** element," using the retired name of the very topic its own Component 4 renames. One of several places the 2028 edition carries from-2021 text unrevised; see flag 9 on [[classes/media-studies/media-studies]].

## ⚠ Provenance

In the from-2021 SoW, Postman and McLuhan are named **only as genealogy** — "can be traced back to." **No idea, book or coinage is attributed to either.** No "the medium is the message," no *Amusing Ourselves to Death*, no global village, no technopoly. In the **2028 SoW they are gone altogether, and no thinker replaces them** — a compulsory 30-mark topic with zero named theory.

Still unattributed in both editions: mediatisation (no Hjarvard or Couldry), convergence (no Jenkins), "public and private personae" (no Goffman). The from-2021 ecology section was the only part of Components 3 and 4 with no external links; the 2028 edition fixes the links (Nielsen Norman, Pew, Ofcom) but not the attribution. See [[analyses/9607-theory-provenance]].
