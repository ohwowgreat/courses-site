# Media Audience

**Key Concept 4.** "How audiences are constructed and addressed by media texts and how audiences interpret and respond to media texts" (Syllabus 2027 p.15).

The concept students think they already know. Its two clauses split the teaching cleanly: audiences as **constructed by industry**, and audiences as **active interpreters**.

## What the syllabus requires

Candidates study (Syllabus 2027 p.15):

- how audiences are grouped and categorised by media industries, including by age, gender and social class, as well as by lifestyle and taste
- how media producers target, attract, reach, address and construct audiences
- how media industries target audiences through the content and appeal of media products and through the ways in which they are marketed, distributed and consumed
- the interrelationship between media technologies and patterns of consumption and response
- how audiences interpret the media, including how they may interpret the same texts in different ways, using a variety of theories or ideologies
- how audiences interact with the media and can be actively involved in media production
- how specialised audiences can be reached, on a local, national and global scale, through different media technologies and platforms
- how media organisations reflect the different needs of mass and specialised audiences, including through targeting
- how audiences use media in different ways, reflecting demographic factors as well as aspects of identity and culture
- the role and significance of specialised audiences, including niche and fan, to the media

## Audiences as constructed

**Categorization** — by age, gender, social class, lifestyle and taste.

**Mode of address** — how a text encourages a reader to recognize themselves as the addressee. The Scheme of Work defines it in the magazine section: "how is a particular audience being addressed by these elements? How is a potential reader encouraged to see her/himself as the addressee?" (SoW p.9). Magazine covers are the ideal teaching text, because the address is on the surface.

**Mass and specialized audiences** — the syllabus's own pairing. The Scheme of Work reaches it via the magazine pitch: "an argument, perhaps, that a niche rather than mass market exists for the publication" (SoW p.12).

**Niche and fan audiences** — named by the syllabus as significant, and reachable globally through platforms.

## Audiences as active interpreters

The syllabus requires that students understand how audiences "may interpret the same texts in different ways, using a variety of theories or ideologies" (p.15) — but names no theory.

The Scheme of Work supplies one, as an **Extension activity** (SoW p.14):

> "Extension activity: explore **Stuart Hall's** ideas of encoding and decoding when making sense of a media text"

See [[entities/stuart-hall]] for the dominant / negotiated / oppositional model. Because it is filed as an Extension activity, Cambridge positions Hall as differentiated content for stronger learners, not as core.

⚠ **The 2028 Scheme of Work drops this extension** (ingested 2026-07-20) — encoding/decoding is suggested nowhere in the new edition, and Hall is recast as a representation theorist. The reception-theory slot for KC4's second clause is now entirely the centre's to fill; the BNDS teaching of the three positions continues as a centre choice.

**The teaching move that works:** take one text the class genuinely disagrees about, then map their actual readings onto the three positions. The theory stops being abstract the moment the students' own argument is the evidence.

## The media diary (from-2021 SoW p.16; relocated in 2028)

⚠ **In the 2028 Scheme of Work the diary moved from Component 2 Section B to Component 4 Section B** (Changing media environments), carried over near-verbatim — same wording, same seven insights, the attention-economy extension updated to "Facebook / X / BlueSky / Snapchat / Instagram." The G11 keep-the-diaries advice below gets stronger, not weaker: the diary is now formally an exercise of the G12 synoptic topic. What C2 Section B gains instead in 2028 is a per-media-area **consumption survey/reflection** (streaming subscriptions, playlist reliance, device preference, "algorithmic influence, fragmentation, niche audience formation, reduced gatekeeping") — see [[concepts/media-industry]].

> "**Exercise: Keep a media diary for one day – list everything you watched, listened to, played, interacted with as well as where you did it, using what technology, whether alone or in company, and for what purpose.**"

Six recorded dimensions: what, where, on what technology, alone or with others, and why — over exactly one day.

Its stated purpose is forward-looking (SoW p.16): "This simple exercise starts to build the foundations for the **Media Ecology** element of **Component 4** – it starts to reveal the '**mediascape**' that we all inhabit." Diaries kept in Grade 11 become the raw contrast material for the Grade 12 synoptic essay. Keep them.

The seven insights Cambridge expects it to surface (SoW p.16): media consumable anywhere on mobile technology; media on demand; limited live viewing except events and sport; social media creating a new communal live experience; news as a stream of updates rather than a fixed form, place and time; "old media" competing and integrating rather than dying; and near-universal availability via piracy.

## The attention economy

The Scheme of Work pushes past description (SoW p.16): "there is an opportunity here to go beyond the descriptive level of experience and to consider conceptual positions, such as 'the attention economy'." The extension asks what it is and "how does it explain how I use Facebook?", then turns to "the actual economy of intellectual and artistic property (which will call forwards to work on regulation in Component 4)."

The component closes with a good short question: **"What media are worth paying for? Why?"** (SoW p.16).

## ⚠ Where the teaching support is thin — partly filled by the 2028 SoW

Most of the syllabus's audience taxonomy was **absent from the from-2021 Scheme of Work's Component 2 section** (pp.13–16): targeting, categorisation, mode of address, mass vs niche, fan audiences, active audiences. "Fan audiences" and "active audiences" appeared nowhere in that document at all.

**The 2028 edition closes much of this.** Fan and audience-agency content is now explicit: "fan and 'prosumer' culture" (C4 Section B focus area), "participatory culture mapping" and "fan labour" (video games area), "audience activism, social media backlash, fan campaigns" (Power topic), target-demographic and audience-segmentation work in every media area's marketing exercise. Mode of address still lives mainly in the C1 magazine material. The consumption-practice orientation remains, but it is now typology-aware.

[[classes/media-studies/lesson-plans/9607-s1-lesson-11-audiences-and-the-portfolio-submission|L11]] was built to supply what the from-2021 support lacked; the 2028 banks now offer Cambridge-authored alternatives worth comparing against it.
