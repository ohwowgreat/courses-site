# Narrative

At AS, narrative is mostly about **expectation** — how an opening plants enigmas, establishes roles, and sets a trajectory. It is not a separate key concept; it sits inside [[concepts/media-language]] and surfaces in both the C1 film-opening brief and Component 2 Section A.

## What Cambridge says

Thin, and worth knowing how thin.

The syllabus mentions narrative twice: media language "develops as genre" and genre is "dynamic and historically relative" (p.14), and — at A Level — "the relationship between postmodernism and narrative" (p.18).

The Scheme of Work's Component 2 section mentions narrative only twice in four pages: openings "set up some **narrative expectations**" (SoW p.13), and the [[entities/roland-barthes]] pointer to "narrative codes" (SoW p.14).

⚠ **No narrative-structure model is named anywhere in any Cambridge document — reconfirmed in the 2028 Scheme of Work** (ingested 2026-07-20). No Todorov, no Propp, no Lévi-Strauss, no three-act structure, no equilibrium/disequilibrium. Barthes' codes remain the only narrative apparatus Cambridge offers — now named directly in the 2028 C1 film row ("Roland Barthes' 5 Narrative Codes") rather than via a link, though the codes themselves are still never listed.

The 2028 edition does add narrative *analysis* work without theory: its editing exercise bank includes a narrative-mapping task (linear vs non-linear sequencing, flashbacks, parallel plotlines, cliffhanger placement) and an ellipsis/time-construction task ("How does editing construct narrative clarity or confusion?"). Structure taught through editing, with the models still ours to supply.

## Where it does appear: enigma

The strongest narrative content in the Cambridge sources is the **enigma**, and it arrives through production rather than theory.

C1 story groundwork (SoW p.10): students "should be able to explain what sort of film this is; who the main character is; what the central conflict is likely to be; **the questions or enigmas that the opening creates** – that is, what information is given? What is withheld? What clues are provided?"

And in the C1 research prompts (CWH p.11): "**how does the film establish an enigma from the outset?**"

So enigma is examined twice over — as a thing to analyze in real openings, and as a thing to construct in the candidate's own. That double duty is why it is the highest-value narrative idea in the AS year.

## The BNDS layer

The Semester 1 scheme teaches a fuller narrative toolkit than Cambridge provides, across two lessons:

**[[classes/media-studies/lesson-plans/9607-s1-lesson-05-narrative-and-the-planning-lock|L05]]** — Todorov's equilibrium, then Propp's character functions, each on a single day, with application turning immediately to students' own C1 plans ("where is the disturbance in your opening"). Success criterion: "Maps characters by function rather than identity and names where Propp strains."

**[[classes/media-studies/lesson-plans/9607-s1-lesson-06-a1-timed-media-language-analysis|L06]]** — Monday and Tuesday "put Propp and Todorov under load on real texts, deliberately hunting for where the theory strains, because strain is what a top-band answer discusses." Propp on a trailer, Todorov across an episode; both days end by logging where the theory failed to fit and why that is analytically interesting.

**Propp and Todorov are centre additions.** They are entirely defensible — the syllabus requires "key theories of media studies" (p.13) without naming any, and Cambridge states it does "not prioritise one theory or approach over another" (p.18). But students should know they are using a chosen framework, not a prescribed one, and examiners will credit the *application*, not the name.

The strain-hunting design is the right instinct: since no model is prescribed, the mark cannot come from reciting one. It comes from showing where a model illuminates a text and where it does not.

## What to watch

The teaching support for the syllabus's own narrative-adjacent bullet — "the significance of challenging and/or subverting conventions" (p.14) — is **absent from the Scheme of Work entirely**; the word "subvert" does not appear in the document. Narrative subversion is a place where the BNDS material has to originate rather than adapt.
