## Goal

This week you take a sign apart and put it back together. You name **connotation** (the words *worried, poor, desperate* from the CS1 exit swap) and use Saussure's terms to separate it from what an image denotes. You will see that connotation is cultural, not natural, using EN/ZH examples. You will study **address** — who a text thinks you are — and commit to the media area you will keep working on until May. Finally you name the caption's power — **anchorage** — and hand in CS2.

## How it runs

### D1 — Mon 09-07: signifier, signified, and the parked list

- **Starter (5 min)** — the L01 parked list on the board, verbatim.
- **Input (15 min)** — from **Deck U2.2**: sign = **signifier** (the form) + **signified** (the idea); **denotation** vs **connotation** on a worked ad reading; **polysemy** — one image, several legitimate readings.
- **Stations (20 min)** — eight images around the room, two columns per image in the sketchpad: *shows* / *suggests*. A "suggests" entry must point at the signifier that triggers it.
- **Close (5 min)** — the theory-card spine starts: use WS 2.1's card template. Two cards tonight — Saussure and Barthes. Every theorist from now on gets a card; the box stays on the shelf until June. CS2 is set now (an everyday text, ≥6 code labels, 30-minute cap).

### D2 — Tue 09-08: connotation is cultural

- **Retrieval (5 min)** — four questions, no notes: signifier / signified / denotation / connotation.
- **Connotation chains (15 min)** — in pairs, build chains from one signifier (rose → romance → Valentine's → spending), then challenge each link: *who taught you that?*

- **EN/ZH divergence (20 min)** — colors and objects whose connotations split by culture: white (purity / mourning), red (danger / fortune), dragons, the number four. The coursebook color wheel (§2.4) is the frame. Add lexicon entries as you go.
- **Written close (5 min)** — using the coursebook §2.1 red-rose frame: two analytical sentences, one denoting, one connoting, with the connotation traced to its culture.

### D3 — Wed 09-09 (double): modes of address, and the commitment

- **Starter (10 min)** — one advertisement on screen, one question: *how old does this text think you are?* Give evidence.
- **Address profiling (40 min)** — three contrasting texts; in small groups build an **implied-reader profile** for each: age, gender, income, knowledge assumed, mood invited, pronouns used. Groups swap and challenge each other's profiles with evidence from the text.
- **Synthesis (20 min)** — formal vs informal address, direct vs indirect; texts don't just find audiences, they *construct* them. Write one paragraph: "This text addresses me as … but I am …"
- **The commitment (15 min)** — commit to your media area, logged on the blog with one line of justification. It feeds the L12 case-study document and stays with you until May. You do not revisit this choice next week.

### D4 — Fri 09-11: anchorage, and CS2

- **Naming the mechanism (10 min)** — L01's Underwood stereograph returns: the caption that told you who the women were has a name — **anchorage** (Barthes): text pinning down which of a polysemic image's meanings you take. **Relay**: when words and image take turns carrying the story.
- **Caption swap (15 min)** — one image, three captions per pair: one anchoring sympathy, one suspicion, one comedy. Read aloud; the image never changed.
- **CS2 (15 min)** — annotations out, peer-checked against the exemplar (six accurate labels is the bar), then collected.
- **Exit (5 min)** — one sentence: "The most powerful caption I saw this week was … because …"
