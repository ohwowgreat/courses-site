## Goal

By the end of week 1 you will use Language, Representation, Industry and Audience as working tools, and hold the course's founding claim — **media is a version, not a window** — with three historical proofs behind it. You prove it by writing the hardest paragraph in the subject: pure denotation, holding back every interpretive impulse. Describing before meaning is what A1 rewards.

## How it runs

### Day 1 — Tue 09-01 (single): the opener and the wager

- **The opener (~30 min)** — introductions, why Media Studies, how the course runs, the two-year map in one slide. Both EN and ZH are legitimate in the room — the shared lexicon starts this week and lives on the wall.
- **The wager (~10 min)** — the claim on the board: *"I'm not impacted by media."* Hands up who agrees; the count is recorded and the vote re-runs in June. Then the homework that will settle it: **the media-use log** (coursebook §1.1) — every media contact over the next 24 hours, however small, EN or ZH, with platform and minutes.
- **Close (~5 min)** — **CS1 announced**: you write it Friday in class, one paragraph, describing only. Blog set-up begins as homework this week (mechanics finish at L07; today just the account).

### Day 2 — Wed 09-02 (double): the four tools and the mediation demo

- **Starter (10 min)** — bring your media-use log; the room's 24 hours are tallied by platform on the board. Everyone here is an **audience** before anything else — the first concept named from your own data.
- **Teach: the four Key Concepts (20 min)** — one shared text read four times through four lenses, applied orally in pairs before anything is written: **Language** (how it is made to mean), **Representation** (how people, places and ideas are shown), **Industry** (who made it, and why), **Audience** (who it is for, and what they do with it). These board anchors stay up all semester.
- **The mediation demo (20 min)** — *a version, not a window*, proven three times over a century before Photoshop:
 - **Staging** — Gardner's *Home of a Rebel Sharpshooter* (1863): the soldier's body was **moved and posed** for the composition, the rifle propped by the photographer. Arranged before the shutter opened.
 - **Selection** — Lange's *Migrant Mother* (1936): one frame chosen from six exposures, cropped, a distracting thumb later retouched away. Every photograph is also the frames not chosen.
 - **Anchorage** — an Underwood & Underwood stereograph (c. 1917): three women watch soldiers march away, and the caption — *"Mother, Wife, and Sweetheart…"* — tells you who they are and how to feel. The words steer the image.

 Then the live turn: one staged classroom "event" is photographed once, and three pairs re-crop and re-caption it into three different stories on their phones. Same event, three versions, five minutes.
- **Card sort (25 min)** — the 24 EN/ZH concept cards in threes, every placement justified aloud; some cards are deliberately contestable ("a WeChat sticker", "the seat you watch from"). Disagreements go on the board.
- **Review (15 min)** — the contested cards argued out; written exit line: *"Media is a version, not a window, because…"*
- **Homework** — **WS 1.1**, the digital-identity mind map (it feeds the L07 blog); the **WS 5.2 body map** is the more visual, lower-language alternative. Lexicon entries continue.

### Day 3 — Fri 09-04 (single): denotation, and CS1

- **Model (15 min)** — a denotation-only paragraph written live on the board about *Migrant Mother*, showing what gets **excluded**: "a woman, seated, hand at her chin; two children face away, heads on her shoulders" survives — "worried", "poor", "desperate" go on a parked list (that list gets its name at L02).
- **CS1 (25 min)** — a new unseen still, one paragraph, denotation only. Sentence frames available: *"The image shows…" / "In the foreground / background…" / "The figure is positioned…" / "The text reads…"* — no evaluative adjectives anywhere.
- **Exit swap (5 min)** — papers trade; each reader circles one smuggled connotation. A paragraph that survives the hunt is the win.
