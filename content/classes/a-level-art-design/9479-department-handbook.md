# 9479 Department Handbook

The student-facing Art & Design handbook, **208 pages, self-authored** — "Written & designed by Doğan Arslanoğlu" (p.206). Ingested 2026-07-19.

It is the most substantial teaching document in the 9479 workspace: visual elements, principles of design, the three components, assessment objectives with checklists, the four disciplines, theme selection, artist studies, the Personal Study, and roughly 87 pages of portfolio samples.

## ⚠ It is old-spec throughout

**This is the finding that matters, because students read this document.**

No "for examination from" statement, version number or date appears anywhere in it. But the structural evidence is unambiguous and internally consistent with the **9479 specification for examination from 2019** — which is *not* the spec this cohort sits.

Every hard number a student could act on — sheet counts, hours, word counts, mark totals — is from the previous specification. The vault's [[classes/a-level-art-design/a-level-art-design|course page]] already warns never to take numbers from the from-2019 support documents; **this handbook belongs on that list**, and unlike the Cambridge support PDFs it is the one students actually hold.

**Recommended:** a revision pass before it is issued again, or at minimum an errata sheet covering the eight rows above. The prose, structure and teaching content are unaffected — this is a numbers problem, not a rewrite.

## Internal contradictions

Independent of the spec issue, three things disagree with themselves:

1. **The Personal Study word count contradicts itself.** pp.36 and 104 say **1000–1500 words**. pp.114–115 say *"an extended essay between **2000 and 3000 words**"* and *"should not exceed 3000 words."* Pages 114–118 read as lifted from a **UK domestic A-level** (AQA/Edexcel/OCR) Personal Study booklet — the tells are "the whole unit," "moderator," the third-person-only instruction, and the claim at p.111 that a missing bibliography means you *"would automatically fail the whole unit,"* which is not attributable to Cambridge from anything in the document.

 Ironically the imported figure lands closer to the **2028-30** requirement (2000–2500) than the handbook's own correct-for-2019 figure does. That is coincidence, not a reason to keep it.

2. **The tint definition contradicts itself.** p.18 distinguishes them properly — *tone* is adding darker shades, *tint* is adding white. The p.19 sub-glossary then says **"tint — Another word for shade,"** which is the opposite. Straightforward error.

3. **Three different AO naming conventions.** p.39 gives the canonical set (Record / Explore / Develop / Present); p.72 relabels them "Record / Experiment and Refine / Develop Ideas / Present a Personal Response" under a header claiming Cambridge alignment; p.84 uses a third set (Recording Ideas / Exploration / Development / Presentation). **Treat p.39 as canonical.**

Also worth knowing: the **Tone and Color entries use British spelling and reference "Figure 0.4 / 0.5"** — figure numbers from another publication. Those two entries appear to be adapted from a Cambridge-endorsed coursebook rather than written locally.

## What is in it

### Visual Elements (pp.7–23)

Six elements, each a definition plus a full-bleed plate: **Line** (Fred Sandback), **Shape** (Escher, *Sky and Water II*), **Space** (Doug Wheeler), **Tone** (Wolfgang Mattheuer), **Color** (pp.18–21, with a CMYK note and six example plates), **Texture**.

Coverage is uneven — Line, Tone and Color get substantial treatment; Shape, Space and Texture get a paragraph each. **Form is never covered** in this chapter, and Texture names "implied, visual texture" as its second type without defining it.

The richest element vocabulary is not here but at **p.81**, buried in the Artist Studies chapter, which adds Form and gives adjective banks for all six — e.g. Texture: "flat, raised, smooth, rough, coarse, shiny, glossy, reflective, scratched, matt, satin, frosted, visible, smooth, blended, impasto, washes, glazes, stippled, splattered, bold, timid, lively, layered."

**No exercises accompany any visual element** — the chapter is definition-plus-plate throughout.

### Principles of Design (pp.24–30) — entirely image-based

⚠ **This section has no text layer at all.** It is invisible to search, cannot be copied, and does not appear in any text extraction. Confirmed by rendering the pages: the content is there, fully designed, just rasterized.

- **p.25 "How to Read Images"** — the four-stage method (Description · Formal Analysis · Interpretation · Judgement) over a grid of **15 paired opposites** for visual communication: balanced/unstable · gestural/technical · sharp/diffuse · symmetry/asymmetry · active/still · distorted/realistic · solitary/juxtaposed · flatness/depth · transparent/opaque · regular/irregular · subtle/amplified · vivid/neutral · unified/fragmented · ordered/loose · light/dark
- **p.26 "17 Key Principles of Design"** — Alignment · Hierarchy · Contrast · Repetition · Proximity · Balance · Color · Space · Emphasis · Proportion · Rhythm · Pattern · Movement · Variety · Unity · Lines · Shapes
- **pp.27–30** — worked visual demonstrations of eight: Emphasis (focal point), Hierarchy, Scale/Proportion (size · ratio · divisions), Contrast (in shape · color · scale · layout), Repetition, Unity & Variety, Balance (symmetrical · asymmetrical)

The 15 paired opposites on p.25 are the most immediately teachable thing in the handbook and the most directly reusable across courses — they are a general apparatus for describing any image.

### The four disciplines (pp.48–57)

**Fine Art** (painting, drawing, sculpture, photography, printmaking, mixed media, assemblage) · **Graphic Communication** (illustration, printmaking, packaging, branding, advertising, signage, typography) · **Three-Dimensional Design** (sculpture, ceramics, product, architecture, interior, environmental, set design, jewellery) · **Textiles and Fashion** (fashion design/illustration, costume, constructed textiles, screen printing, batik, digital-printed textiles, surface pattern).

Candidates may focus on one **or combine several**. Two distinctions worth noting:

- **Fine Art responds to "a theme" only**; the other three respond to "a theme **or brief**." Consistent across pp.50–57.
- **3-D Design uniquely requires** consideration of "the setting in which the final outcome will be situated, e.g. interior/exterior, urban/rural" (p.55).

Each discipline's Knowledge and Understanding list repeats **six identical bullets**; only the discipline-specific ones differ.

### Themes (pp.66–72)

BNDS-authored guidance on *choosing* a theme — **not a list of past Cambridge themes**, which the handbook does not contain anywhere.

Nine "qualities of an appropriate theme" (personal relevance · breadth and depth · creativity and originality · flexibility · researchability · visual and conceptual interest · educational value · relevance to the assessment objectives · engagement with broader issues), then a seven-step selection guide, then the assignment itself: complete a theme selection form for **at least four possible themes** with related artists and possible artworks, then choose one and justify it. Submitted by the end of the week.

Explicitly Component 1-facing (p.66).

### Assessment Objectives (pp.38–47)

The four AOs at p.39 with student-facing bullet guidance, then a two-page overview-and-checklist spread each. The checklists are genuinely usable — AO1's runs seven questions from "collected images to show your inspiration and stimuli?" through "clearly linked all of your work to your starting point?"

⚠ **No AO weightings or percentages appear anywhere.** The assessment-overview tables (p.33), Cambridge rubric (p.101), assessment criteria (p.113) and assignment rubrics (pp.72, 95) all announce tables that exist only as images.

✅ **Resolved 2026-07-19: take the assessment objectives and all rubrics from the 2028-30 syllabus, not from this handbook.** The handbook's AO wording is the old four-AO set and its rubric tables are unrecoverable images — so the syllabus is both the correct authority and the only usable one. The handbook's AO *checklists* (pp.41–47) remain useful as student-facing scaffolding, provided the objectives they hang off come from the syllabus.

### Personal Study (pp.104–118)

Three study types offered (p.105): **Technical** (media, surfaces, tools, techniques), **Stylistic** (signs, symbols, metaphor, meaning, figurative and formal elements), **Contextual** (art and society, social/historical/cultural contexts, theories of art) — combinable.

The integration rule (p.105) is stated forcefully and is worth keeping regardless of spec: *"the study will form the starting point of your practical coursework. The two must link together and inform each other i.e. they are not separate elements but dependent on each other. Your success depends on this point."*

⚠ This chapter carries the word-count contradiction and the imported UK-board material described above.

### Portfolio samples (pp.119–205)

Roughly 87 pages of sample work, captioned only ("Final Outcome", "SAMPLE 1 / Component 1"). No text layer. Not catalogued here — the pages are the content.
