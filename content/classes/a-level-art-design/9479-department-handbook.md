# 9479 Department Handbook

The student-facing Art & Design handbook, 208 pages — "Written & designed by Doğan Arslanoğlu" (p.206). It covers the visual elements, principles of design, the three components, assessment objectives with checklists, the four disciplines, theme selection, artist studies, the Personal Study, and around 87 pages of portfolio samples.

> [!important] Some numbers in the handbook are out of date
> Several hard figures in the handbook — sheet counts, supervised-test hours, mark totals and word counts — come from an earlier version of the 9479 specification, not the one you sit. Do not rely on those numbers. For current figures, use the syllabus. In particular:
> - **Personal Study / written analysis: 2000–2500 words** (the handbook's 1000–1500 is out of date).
> - **Assessment objectives and all marking rubrics: use the 2028-30 syllabus**, not the handbook. The handbook's AO checklists (pp.41–47) are still useful as scaffolding, but the objectives and rubrics themselves come from the syllabus.

A few smaller corrections:
- **Tint vs tone:** tone is adding darker shades; tint is adding white (p.18). The p.19 sub-glossary entry "tint — another word for shade" is an error.
- **AO names:** treat p.39 as the correct set — Record · Explore · Develop · Present. Pages 72 and 84 use different labels.

## What is in it

### Visual Elements (pp.7–23)

Six elements, each a definition plus a full-bleed plate: **Line** (Fred Sandback), **Shape** (Escher, *Sky and Water II*), **Space** (Doug Wheeler), **Tone** (Wolfgang Mattheuer), **Color** (pp.18–21, with a CMYK note and six example plates), **Texture**. Form is not covered in this chapter.

The fullest element vocabulary is at **p.81**, in the Artist Studies chapter, which adds Form and gives adjective banks for all six — e.g. Texture: "flat, raised, smooth, rough, coarse, shiny, glossy, reflective, scratched, matt, satin, frosted, visible, smooth, blended, impasto, washes, glazes, stippled, splattered, bold, timid, lively, layered."

### Principles of Design (pp.24–30)

- **p.25 "How to Read Images"** — the four-stage method (Description · Formal Analysis · Interpretation · Judgement) over a grid of **15 paired opposites**: balanced/unstable · gestural/technical · sharp/diffuse · symmetry/asymmetry · active/still · distorted/realistic · solitary/juxtaposed · flatness/depth · transparent/opaque · regular/irregular · subtle/amplified · vivid/neutral · unified/fragmented · ordered/loose · light/dark
- **p.26 "17 Key Principles of Design"** — Alignment · Hierarchy · Contrast · Repetition · Proximity · Balance · Color · Space · Emphasis · Proportion · Rhythm · Pattern · Movement · Variety · Unity · Lines · Shapes
- **pp.27–30** — worked visual demonstrations of eight: Emphasis (focal point), Hierarchy, Scale/Proportion (size · ratio · divisions), Contrast (in shape · color · scale · layout), Repetition, Unity & Variety, Balance (symmetrical · asymmetrical)

### The four disciplines (pp.48–57)

**Fine Art** (painting, drawing, sculpture, photography, printmaking, mixed media, assemblage) · **Graphic Communication** (illustration, printmaking, packaging, branding, advertising, signage, typography) · **Three-Dimensional Design** (sculpture, ceramics, product, architecture, interior, environmental, set design, jewellery) · **Textiles and Fashion** (fashion design/illustration, costume, constructed textiles, screen printing, batik, digital-printed textiles, surface pattern).

You may focus on one discipline or combine several. Two distinctions:

- **Fine Art responds to a theme only**; the other three respond to a theme **or a brief** (pp.50–57).
- **3-D Design also requires** you to consider "the setting in which the final outcome will be situated, e.g. interior/exterior, urban/rural" (p.55).

### Themes (pp.66–72)

Guidance on *choosing* a theme (this is Component 1-facing, p.66). Nine qualities of an appropriate theme — personal relevance · breadth and depth · creativity and originality · flexibility · researchability · visual and conceptual interest · educational value · relevance to the assessment objectives · engagement with broader issues — then a seven-step selection guide.

The assignment: complete a theme selection form for **at least four possible themes** with related artists and possible artworks, then choose one and justify it. Submitted by the end of the week.

### Assessment Objectives (pp.38–47)

The four AOs at p.39 with student-facing bullet guidance, then a two-page overview-and-checklist spread for each. The checklists are usable — AO1's runs seven questions, from "collected images to show your inspiration and stimuli?" through "clearly linked all of your work to your starting point?" Take the objectives and rubrics themselves from the 2028-30 syllabus.

### Personal Study (pp.104–118)

Three study types (p.105), which can be combined: **Technical** (media, surfaces, tools, techniques), **Stylistic** (signs, symbols, metaphor, meaning, figurative and formal elements), **Contextual** (art and society, social/historical/cultural contexts, theories of art).

The integration rule (p.105): *"the study will form the starting point of your practical coursework. The two must link together and inform each other i.e. they are not separate elements but dependent on each other. Your success depends on this point."*

Use the 2000–2500 word count for the written analysis, not the figures printed in this chapter.

### Portfolio samples (pp.119–205)

Around 87 pages of sample work, captioned only ("Final Outcome", "SAMPLE 1 / Component 1").
