## Goal

This session moves from experiments to a finished work. The question: *what is your collage about?* Not what is in it — what it **says**. Every technique from L10–11 serves one **main idea**, written in one sentence before you start composing. You then make two different drafts of the same idea, because the first arrangement is rarely the strongest.

## How it runs

- **Teach: meaning (15 min)** — **Romare Bearden**: *The Block*, *Three Folk Musicians* — collage as memory and neighborhood, cut paper standing in for a whole world. The pre-photography ancestors make the same point: a Dutch **vanitas** heaps books, globes and jewels together to say *none of this lasts* — objects juxtaposed into a sentence, 1662. Composition is how the sentence gets its grammar.

- **Main idea (10 min)** — write one sentence in your sketchbook: "My collage is about … " — concrete beats abstract ("my grandmother's kitchen" beats "memory"). An accessible fallback is the **silhouette self-portrait**: your profile filled with what fills your head. State your idea aloud at your table; the teacher initials each.
- **Studio: two drafts (45 min)** — make two dry-fit compositions of the same idea on separate boards, deliberately different (one crowded / one sparse, one symmetrical / one tilted). No glue today. Photograph both. Then circle one and write your reason.
- **CS3 + close (20 min)** — **CS3 collected**: the experiment set (juxtaposition, scale, layering, text-and-image + home experiments), checked for range and honest annotation. The final collage is made in class W14–W15 from your circled draft, marked on **juxtaposition, layering, composition, meaning**; the format will be confirmed in class. Post on MB.
