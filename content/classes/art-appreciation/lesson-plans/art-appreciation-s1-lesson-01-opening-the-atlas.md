## Goal

Three days doing one job: getting everybody to look before they explain. The atlas method is genuinely strange to students taught art history as a timeline — say the strange thing out loud on day one, keep saying it, and do not apologise for it. The darkroom is on day three because it is the fastest way to make an image stop being a screen and become a thing that light did to a surface, a distinction that is load-bearing for the whole of Unit 4 in December.

## How it runs

- **Day 1 · Tue 1 Sept · `U1·L1`** — **first half:** First Session Opener — introductions, getting to know the group, why this subject, how the course runs. Mixed G11 + G12, so pair across the year groups deliberately; some of this room already knows each other and some does not. **Second half:** the atlas method and how you are marked — Warburg's panels and why resemblance beats date (kept tight), the course map with the four majors and the retrospective on one sheet, left up. Close: each student names one thing about this course that sounds wrong to them.
- **Day 2 · Wed 2 Sept · `U1·L2`** — look before you explain. Ten minutes silent on one image with the title withheld; describe round written for two minutes, then spoken. Close: what did someone else see that you did not.
- **Day 3 · Thu 3 Sept · `U1·L3`** — darkroom: the image as physical trace. Twenty-five minutes making, fifteen writing the reflection while the print is still wet. CS1 collected at the door.

## Assessment

- **CS1** — darkroom reflection, written in the room, collected Fri 2026-09-04. Marked on naming a physical decision rather than a feeling about the result.
